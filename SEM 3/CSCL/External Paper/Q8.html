(a) Compare and contrast between IDS(Intrusion Detection System) and IPS(Intrusion Prevention System). 

Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS) are critical components of network security, but they serve different roles and have distinct functionalities. Here's a comparison and contrast between the two:

Intrusion Detection System (IDS)

Function: An IDS monitors network and/or system activities for malicious activities or policy violations. It typically works in a passive mode, analyzing copies of network traffic.

Response to Threats: When an IDS detects a potential threat, it logs the information and alerts system administrators. However, it does not take action to stop the threat.

Deployment: IDS can be network-based (NIDS), monitoring traffic from all devices on the network, or host-based (HIDS), monitoring activity on a single host.

Detection Methods:

  • Signature-Based Detection: Detects known threats by looking for specific patterns, such as byte sequences in network traffic, or known malicious instruction sequences.
  • Anomaly-Based Detection: Detects unknown threats by comparing current activity to a baseline and looking for anomalies.

Pros:

  • Effective in identifying known threats.
  • Provides detailed information about detected intrusions.

Cons:

  • Cannot prevent detected threats.
  • High false positive rate, especially in anomaly-based detection.

Intrusion Prevention System (IPS)

Function: An IPS, similar to an IDS, monitors network and/or system activities, but it can also actively block or prevent intrusions.

Response to Threats: When an IPS identifies a potential threat, it can take action to block the threat, such as dropping malicious packets, resetting connections, or blocking traffic from the source IP address.

Deployment: Like IDS, IPS can be network-based or host-based. It is typically placed inline (directly in the traffic flow) to actively monitor and respond to network traffic.

Detection Methods: Uses similar methods as IDS (signature-based and anomaly-based detection), but also includes other techniques like stateful inspection.

Pros:

  • Can prevent or mitigate attacks in real-time.
  • Reduces the burden on security teams by automatically responding to threats.

Cons:

  • Risk of false positives leading to legitimate traffic being blocked.
  • Can become a point of failure in the network if not properly managed.

Comparison and Contrast

Active vs Passive: The primary difference is that IDS is passive and only detects and alerts, while IPS is active and can prevent or mitigate attacks.

Placement in Network: IPS is usually placed inline to actively monitor and control the traffic, whereas IDS is typically deployed out of band.

False Positives: Both systems can generate false positives, but the consequences are more significant in IPS as it can lead to legitimate traffic being blocked.

Complexity and Management: IPS is generally more complex to manage due to its active role in network traffic. Misconfigurations can have a direct impact on network performance.

Use Case: IDS is suitable for environments where detecting threats and understanding attack patterns are crucial but active intervention is not desired, while IPS is ideal for environments where preventing known threats is a priority.

In summary, IDS and IPS are complementary technologies, with IDS suited for monitoring and identifying security breaches and IPS aimed at actively preventing and mitigating attacks. The choice between IDS and IPS, or the decision to use both, depends on the specific security needs and policies of the organization.

(b) What are the various intrusion detection methodologies? Also, explain any three types of threats.

Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) employ various methodologies to detect unauthorized access or anomalies in a network or system. The primary intrusion detection methodologies are:

Signature-Based Detection:

  • Description: This method relies on a database of known threat signatures - patterns of data that are typical of known malicious activities.
  • Operation: The IDS compares incoming traffic against these signatures to identify potential threats.
  • Pros: Highly effective at detecting known threats.
  • Cons: Cannot detect new, unknown threats (zero-day attacks).

Anomaly-Based Detection:

  • Description: This approach involves establishing a baseline of normal network behavior and then monitoring for deviations from this norm.
  • Operation: It uses machine learning algorithms or statistical analysis to detect unusual patterns that may indicate a security breach.
  • Pros: Capable of detecting novel or unknown threats.
  • Cons: Higher false positive rates as benign activities that are not typical may be flagged as threats.

Stateful Protocol Analysis:

  • Description: This method interprets various protocols and data transfers to determine if they are behaving as expected.
  • Operation: It understands and tracks the state of network protocols over a session, ensuring they adhere to the defined protocol standards.
  • Pros: Effective in identifying protocol-specific attacks and anomalies.
  • Cons: Requires deep knowledge of protocols and can be resource-intensive.

Heuristic-Based Detection:

  • Description: Uses algorithms to evaluate the behavior of applications and network traffic, looking for unusual patterns.
  • Operation: It identifies potentially harmful software or activity by comparing it to known harmful behaviors.
  • Pros: Good at detecting new threats that haven't been encountered before.
  • Cons: Risk of false positives is high due to the heuristic nature of detection.

Types of Threats

Network Attacks:

  • Description: Attacks aimed at disrupting, damaging, or gaining unauthorized access to computer networks and systems.
  • Examples: DDoS attacks, Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) attacks, and packet sniffing.
  • Concerns: These attacks can compromise network integrity, steal data, or render network services unavailable.

Malware:

  • Description: Malicious software designed to harm, exploit, or take unauthorized control over a system.
  • Examples: Viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware.
  • Concerns: Malware can lead to data theft, system damage, spying, and other malicious activities.

Insider Threats:

  • Description: Threats from people within the organization who have inside information concerning its security practices, data, and computer systems.
  • Examples: Employees misusing access privileges, stealing confidential information, or sabotaging systems.
  • Concerns: These threats are hard to detect as insiders already have authorized access, and their actions can lead to significant damage or data breaches.

Each of these threats requires specific strategies and tools to detect and mitigate, emphasizing the need for a multi-layered approach to security that includes robust intrusion detection capabilities.
