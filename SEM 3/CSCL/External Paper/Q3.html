(a) Discuss the various type of deliberate software attacks designed to damage, destroy or deny service to target systems?

Virus Attacks: Viruses are malicious programs that attach themselves to clean files and spread throughout a computer system, corrupting files and affecting the performance of a system. They often require some form of user action to activate and spread.

Worm Attacks: Worms are similar to viruses, but they can spread autonomously without human intervention. They exploit vulnerabilities in software or operating systems and can cause widespread damage by replicating rapidly across networks.

Trojan Horse Attacks: Trojans disguise themselves as legitimate software but perform malicious actions when executed. They can steal data, spy on users, or create backdoors for further attacks.

Ransomware Attacks: Ransomware encrypts a user's data and demands payment for the decryption key. These attacks can lock users out of their systems and can cause significant data loss.

Denial-of-Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) Attacks: DoS and DDoS attacks aim to overwhelm a system's resources or the bandwidth of a network, making the service inaccessible to legitimate users. DDoS attacks use multiple compromised systems to launch the attack.

SQL Injection Attacks: These attacks target databases through web applications. Attackers manipulate standard SQL queries to gain unauthorized access to data, modify database information, and execute administrative operations on databases.

Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) Attacks: In XSS attacks, attackers inject malicious scripts into webpages viewed by other users. This can lead to hijacking of user sessions, defacing websites, or redirecting the user to malicious sites.

Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Attacks: These occur when attackers intercept and alter the communication between two parties without their knowledge, often in unsecured public Wi-Fi networks.

Zero-Day Exploits: These attacks exploit vulnerabilities in software or hardware that are unknown to the vendor or have no patch available at the time of the attack.

(b) What are the various essential challenges in mobile security?

Device Loss or Theft: Mobile devices are portable and easily lost or stolen, leading to potential unauthorized access to sensitive information.

Insecure Wi-Fi Networks: Mobile users often connect to public Wi-Fi networks, which may not be secure, exposing them to risks like MitM attacks.

Vulnerabilities in Mobile Operating Systems: Like any software, mobile OS can have vulnerabilities that attackers can exploit.

Phishing Attacks: Mobile users are increasingly targeted by phishing attacks, often through emails or malicious apps, due to the smaller screen size and simplified user interface which can make fraudulent content less noticeable.

Malware and Viruses: The proliferation of mobile apps has increased the risk of downloading apps containing malware or viruses.

Data Leakage: Unintentional data leakage due to app permissions, where apps access more data than needed, can be a significant risk.

Outdated Software: Not regularly updating the mobile OS and apps can leave security vulnerabilities unpatched.

Unsecured Apps: Downloading apps from unofficial or unsecured sources can increase the risk of installing malicious software.

IoT Integration: As mobile devices increasingly integrate with other IoT devices, the security of these interconnected systems can become a concern.

User Negligence: Often, the biggest security risk comes from the users themselves, whether through weak passwords, sharing sensitive information, or lack of awareness about security practices.
