Identity management

Identity management, also known as identity and access management (IAM), refers to the processes, technologies, and policies used to manage and control digital identities within an organization. It involves managing user identities, their authentication, authorization, and access to resources and systems.

Identity management encompasses various aspects, including:

  1. User Provisioning: User provisioning involves creating, modifying, and disabling user accounts and their associated access rights. It ensures that users have the appropriate access privileges based on their roles and responsibilities within the organization.
  2. Authentication: Authentication is the process of verifying the identity of users accessing a system or application. It typically involves the use of usernames and passwords, but can also include additional factors such as biometrics, smart cards, or one-time passwords for stronger security.
  3. Authorization: Authorization determines what resources and actions a user is allowed to access or perform within a system or application. It is based on the user's authenticated identity and their assigned roles, permissions, or attributes.
  4. Single Sign-On (SSO): SSO enables users to authenticate once and gain access to multiple systems or applications without the need to re-enter their credentials. It improves user experience and reduces the burden of managing multiple passwords.
  5. Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): RBAC is a method of managing access rights based on predefined roles. Users are assigned specific roles, and their access privileges are determined by the permissions associated with those roles. RBAC simplifies access management by grouping users with similar responsibilities.
  6. Identity Federation: Identity federation allows users to access resources across different organizations or domains using their existing credentials. It establishes trust relationships between identity providers and service providers, enabling seamless and secure access to shared resources.
  7. Password Management: Password management involves enforcing strong password policies, such as password complexity requirements, expiration periods, and password history. It may also include self-service password reset mechanisms and multi-factor authentication to enhance security.
  8. Audit and Compliance: Identity management systems often include auditing capabilities to track user activities, access requests, and changes to user identities and permissions. This helps organizations meet regulatory compliance requirements and detect any unauthorized or suspicious activities.
  9. Identity Lifecycle Management: Identity lifecycle management encompasses the entire lifecycle of a user's identity within an organization, from onboarding to offboarding. It includes processes for user registration, account provisioning, access rights management, and deprovisioning when users leave the organization.
  10. Identity Governance: Identity governance focuses on defining and enforcing policies and controls related to identity management. It ensures that access rights are granted based on business needs, compliance requirements, and least privilege principles.
