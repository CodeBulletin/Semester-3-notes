(a) What is key-logging, and what are various types of key-loggers can you Provide an in-depth explanation of how each type of key-logger operates? Additionally, what tool can be used to detect the presence of key-loggers

What is Keylogging?

Keylogging, also known as keystroke logging, involves recording the keys struck on a keyboard, typically covertly, to monitor and record user actions. It is commonly used in cyberattacks to capture sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, and personal messages.

Types of Keyloggers and Their Operations

Hardware Keyloggers:

  • Operation: These are physical devices plugged into a computer between the keyboard and the PC port. They record keystrokes directly from the keyboard.
  • Detection: Hardware keyloggers can be detected by physically checking for unknown devices connected to the keyboard port.

Software Keyloggers:

  • Kernel-Based: Operates at the kernel level of the OS, capturing keystrokes directly from the input subsystem. It's challenging to detect due to its deep integration.
  • API-Based: Uses application programming interfaces to monitor keyboard inputs. It can capture specific information like window titles.
  • Form Grabbing-Based: Records form data before it's passed over the Internet. Often used in capturing web form data like login credentials.
  • JavaScript-Based: Deployed on websites, capturing keystrokes as they occur in form fields on web pages.
  • Detection for Software Keyloggers: Software-based keyloggers can be detected using anti-malware or anti-keylogger software, which scans for suspicious activities or known keylogging programs.

Tools for Detecting Keyloggers

  • Anti-Malware Software: Many comprehensive anti-malware solutions have capabilities to detect both hardware and software keyloggers.
  • Anti-Keylogger Software: Specialized software designed specifically to detect and remove keylogging software.
  • Security Analysis Tools: Some advanced users may use security and system analysis tools to monitor system processes and network traffic for signs of keylogging activities.

(b) Compare and contrast a virus from & Worm, Elaborate any four different virus and their implications on system security.

Comparison

  • Propagation: Viruses require human action to replicate (like opening a file), whereas worms can self-replicate and spread independently.
  • Attachment: Viruses attach themselves to files and require a host for spreading. Worms are standalone software and do not need to attach to other programs.

Examples of Viruses and Their Implications

ILOVEYOU Virus:

  • Nature: A computer virus that spread via email.
  • Impact: Overwrote files and spread itself by hijacking the victim's email system to send itself to contacts.

Conficker Virus:

  • Nature: A virus that exploits a vulnerability in Windows OS.
  • Impact: Forms a botnet; used for spamming and installing malicious software.

Slammer Worm:

  • Nature: A fast-spreading worm targeting Microsoft SQL 2000 servers.
  • Impact: Caused widespread internet outages and affected banking systems.

Stuxnet Worm:

  • Nature: A sophisticated worm targeting industrial control systems.
  • Impact: Damaged Iran's nuclear program; highlighted the potential for cyber warfare.

(c) Explain tradition techniques of attacks on wireless network

  • Eavesdropping: Interception of communications over wireless networks. Attackers capture unencrypted wireless data, gaining access to sensitive information.
  • Unauthorized Access: Gaining access to a wireless network without permission, often by breaking encryption or using default passwords.
  • Man-in-the-Middle Attacks: Intercepting and altering the communication between two parties in a wireless network.
  • Spoofing: Creating a rogue wireless access point that mimics a legitimate one, tricking users into connecting to it.
  • Denial-of-Service Attacks: Flooding a wireless network with traffic to make it unavailable to its intended users.
  • Evil Twin Attacks: Setting up a rogue Wi-Fi access point with a name similar to a legitimate network, tricking users into connecting and capturing their data.
