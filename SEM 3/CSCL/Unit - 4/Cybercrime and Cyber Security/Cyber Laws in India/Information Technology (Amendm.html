Information Technology (Amendment) Act, 2008

The Information Technology (Amendment) Act, 2008, is a significant amendment to the original Information Technology Act, 2000, in India. This amendment was introduced to address the emerging challenges and loopholes in the field of information technology and cyber law. Here are the key aspects of the Information Technology (Amendment) Act, 2008:

Expanded Definition of Cyber Crimes: The amendment expanded the definition of cyber crimes to include acts like identity theft, cheating by impersonation using computer resources, violation of privacy, and cyber terrorism.

Introduction of Section 66A: This controversial section was introduced to penalize sending offensive messages through communication services. However, it was later struck down by the Supreme Court of India in 2015 for being vague and unconstitutional.

Data Protection and Privacy: The amendment introduced specific sections (43A and 72A) related to data protection and privacy. These sections hold corporate bodies responsible for implementing reasonable security practices to protect data and impose a penalty for breach of confidentiality and privacy.

Recognition of Electronic Signatures: Beyond digital signatures, the amendment recognized electronic signatures to authenticate electronic records, broadening the scope of legally recognized electronic authentication methods.

Establishment of the Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In): The amendment formalized the role of CERT-In as a national agency for incident response. CERT-In is responsible for dealing with cyber security threats like hacking and phishing.

Intermediary Liability: The amendment introduced specific provisions related to the liability of intermediaries. Intermediaries, like ISPs and social media platforms, are required to exercise due diligence and not knowingly host or publish information that is harmful, misleading, or unlawful.

Amendments to the Indian Penal Code, Evidence Act, and Banker’s Book Evidence Act: The act also made consequential amendments to the Indian Penal Code, the Indian Evidence Act, and the Banker’s Book Evidence Act, enabling these laws to be applicable for electronic records and communications.

The Information Technology (Amendment) Act, 2008, played a crucial role in enhancing the legal framework for information technology in India. However, it has also faced criticism and challenges, particularly regarding aspects like data privacy, freedom of expression, and the broad powers given to the government for surveillance and interception of digital communications.
