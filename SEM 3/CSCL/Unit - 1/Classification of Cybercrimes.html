Classification of Cybercrimes

1. Cybercrime against individual: 

  1. E-Mail Spoofing and Other Online Fraud: E-mail spoofing is a technique used by cybercriminals to forge the sender's address in an email to make it appear as if it is coming from a different source. This is often done to deceive recipients into believing the email is legitimate and to trick them into revealing sensitive information or performing certain actions. Online fraud encompasses various fraudulent activities conducted over the internet, such as identity theft, advance-fee fraud, or online scams that aim to deceive individuals or organizations for financial gain.
  2. Phishing: Phishing is a type of cyber attack where cybercriminals send fraudulent emails, messages, or create fake websites that appear to be from reputable sources. The goal is to trick recipients into providing sensitive information, such as passwords, credit card details, or social security numbers. Phishing attacks often use social engineering techniques to manipulate victims into taking actions that benefit the attacker.
  3. Spamming: Spamming refers to the mass distribution of unsolicited and often unwanted messages, typically through email. These messages can contain advertisements, scams, or malicious content. Spamming is not only a nuisance but can also be used to spread malware, phishing attempts, or fraudulent schemes.
  4. Cyberdefamation: Cyberdefamation involves the act of defaming or damaging someone's reputation through online platforms, such as social media, websites, or forums. It can include false statements, rumors, or malicious content that harms an individual's or organization's reputation. Cyberdefamation can have serious consequences for the victim, including personal and professional damage.
  5. Cyberstalking and Harassment: Cyberstalking refers to the persistent and unwanted pursuit or harassment of an individual through online channels, such as social media, email, or messaging platforms. It involves repeated threats, intimidation, or monitoring of the victim's online activities. Cyber harassment encompasses a broader range of online harassment, including cyberbullying, hate speech, or the dissemination of explicit or private content without consent.
  6. Computer Sabotage: Computer sabotage involves intentional actions to disrupt or damage computer systems, networks, or data. This can include spreading malware, launching denial-of-service (DoS) attacks to overwhelm systems, or tampering with critical infrastructure. Computer sabotage can have severe consequences, including financial losses, service disruptions, or compromise of sensitive information.

2. Cybercrime against property:

  1. Credit Card Frauds: Credit card fraud refers to the unauthorized use of someone else's credit card information to make fraudulent transactions or obtain goods or services without the cardholder's consent. This can involve stealing physical credit cards, obtaining card details through phishing or hacking, or using counterfeit cards. Credit card fraud can result in financial losses for the cardholder and can also impact businesses and financial institutions.
  2. Intellectual Property Crime: Intellectual property (IP) crime involves the infringement or unauthorized use of intellectual property rights, such as copyrights, trademarks, or patents. This can include activities like counterfeiting, piracy, or unauthorized distribution of copyrighted material, such as movies, music, software, or counterfeit products. IP crime not only causes financial losses to creators and rights holders but also undermines innovation, creativity, and fair competition.
  3. Internet Time Theft: Internet time theft refers to the unauthorized use of an employer's internet resources or time for personal purposes during work hours. This can include activities like excessive personal internet browsing, accessing social media, or engaging in online shopping while on the job. Internet time theft can result in decreased productivity, wasted resources, and potential security risks for organizations.

3. Cybercrime against organization: 

  1. Unauthorized Accessing of Computer: Unauthorized accessing of a computer refers to gaining unauthorized entry into a computer system, network, or device without proper authorization. This can involve bypassing security measures, exploiting vulnerabilities, or using stolen credentials to gain access to sensitive information or perform malicious activities.
  2. Password Sniffing: Password sniffing is a technique used by cybercriminals to intercept and capture passwords or login credentials as they are transmitted over a network. This can be done by monitoring network traffic or using specialized software or hardware devices. Password sniffing allows attackers to gain unauthorized access to user accounts or sensitive information.
  3. Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attacks: Denial-of-Service attacks aim to disrupt or disable the normal functioning of a computer system, network, or website by overwhelming it with a flood of traffic or requests. This prevents legitimate users from accessing the targeted resource. Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks involve multiple compromised devices, forming a botnet, to launch the attack.
  4. Virus: A virus is a type of malicious software that can replicate itself and spread from one computer to another. It typically attaches itself to legitimate programs or files and can cause damage by corrupting or deleting data, disrupting system operations, or spreading to other systems. Viruses often require user interaction or the execution of infected files to propagate.
  5. E-Mail Bombing: E-Mail bombing involves sending a large volume of emails to a targeted individual or organization, overwhelming their email server and causing disruption. This can result in the recipient's inbox being flooded with unwanted emails, making it difficult to receive or send legitimate messages.
  6. Salami Attack: A salami attack is a type of cybercrime where small, unnoticed amounts of money or data are fraudulently taken from multiple accounts or transactions. The attacker accumulates these small amounts over time, similar to slicing thin pieces from a salami, hence the name.
  7. Logic Bomb: A logic bomb is a piece of code or software that is intentionally inserted into a system to execute a malicious action when specific conditions are met. It remains dormant until triggered by a predefined event or date. Logic bombs can be used to delete data, disrupt operations, or cause other malicious activities.
  8. Trojan Horse: A Trojan horse is a type of malware that disguises itself as legitimate software or files to deceive users into executing or installing it. Once activated, it can perform various malicious actions, such as stealing sensitive information, providing unauthorized access to the attacker, or damaging the system.
  9. Data Diddling: Data diddling involves unauthorized manipulation or alteration of data before, during, or after it is entered into a computer system. This can be done to commit fraud, conceal activities, or cause financial harm by modifying or deleting critical information.
  10. Industrial Spying: Industrial spying, also known as corporate espionage, refers to the unauthorized gathering of confidential or proprietary information from a competitor or another organization for competitive advantage. This can involve hacking into computer systems, stealing trade secrets, or conducting surveillance to gain valuable insights or intellectual property.
  11. Computer Network Intrusions: Computer network intrusions involve unauthorized access or intrusion into computer networks, systems, or devices. Intruders may exploit vulnerabilities, use stolen credentials, or employ various techniques to gain unauthorized access to sensitive information, disrupt operations, or carry out malicious activities.
  12. Software Piracy: Software piracy refers to the unauthorized copying, distribution, or use of copyrighted software without the permission of the software's owner. This can involve downloading or sharing software from unauthorized sources, using cracked or counterfeit software, or bypassing licensing restrictions. Software piracy deprives software developers of revenue and violates intellectual property rights.

4. Cybercrime against society: 

  1. Forgery: Forgery refers to the act of creating or altering documents, objects, or information with the intent to deceive or defraud others. In the context of cybercrime, forgery can involve creating fake digital documents, such as counterfeit identification cards, passports, or financial records. It can also include forging digital signatures or manipulating electronic records to misrepresent information or gain unauthorized access to systems.
  2. Cyberterrorism: Cyberterrorism involves the use of cyber attacks by individuals or groups with political, ideological, or religious motivations to create fear, panic, or disruption on a large scale. Cyberterrorists may target critical infrastructure, government networks, or financial institutions with the aim of causing significant damage, disruption, or loss of life. Their actions can include hacking, spreading malware, launching denial-of-service attacks, or disseminating propaganda to further their political or ideological objectives.
  3. Web Jacking: Web jacking, also known as website hijacking, refers to the unauthorized takeover or control of a website or web server by an attacker. This can involve exploiting vulnerabilities in the website's security, gaining unauthorized access to the server, or manipulating the website's content or functionality. Web jacking can be done for various reasons, such as defacing the website, stealing sensitive information, or using the compromised website for malicious activities.

5. Crimes emanating from Usenet newsgroup: 

Crimes emanating from Usenet newsgroups refer to illegal activities that are conducted through Usenet, a decentralized network of discussion forums and message boards. While Usenet itself is a legitimate platform for exchanging information and engaging in discussions, it can also be misused for criminal purposes. Here are some examples of crimes that can occur on Usenet:

Distribution of Illegal Content: Usenet newsgroups can be used to distribute illegal content, such as copyrighted material, pirated software, child pornography, or other illicit materials. Users may share or download these materials through Usenet, which is a violation of copyright laws and can lead to legal consequences.

Fraud and Scams: Usenet can be a breeding ground for various types of fraud and scams. This can include phishing attempts, where users are tricked into revealing personal information or login credentials, or fraudulent schemes that promise financial gains or products that are never delivered. Scammers may use Usenet to target unsuspecting individuals and carry out their fraudulent activities.

Harassment and Hate Speech: Usenet newsgroups can be used as platforms for harassment, hate speech, or the dissemination of offensive or harmful content. Users may engage in cyberbullying, spread discriminatory messages, or incite violence or hatred towards specific individuals or groups. Such activities are not only morally wrong but can also be illegal in many jurisdictions.

Cyberstalking: Usenet can be misused for cyberstalking, where individuals are targeted with persistent and unwanted online attention, threats, or harassment. Cyberstalkers may use Usenet newsgroups to track their victims, gather personal information, or publicly harass them.
