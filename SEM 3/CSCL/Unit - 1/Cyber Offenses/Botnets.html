Botnets

Botnets are networks of compromised computers or devices that are under the control of a malicious actor, known as the botmaster or bot herder. These compromised devices, often referred to as "bots" or "zombies," are typically infected with malware that allows the botmaster to remotely control them without the knowledge or consent of their owners.

Botnets are created by infecting a large number of devices, which can include computers, servers, smartphones, IoT devices, or even routers. The malware used to infect these devices is often spread through various means, such as phishing emails, malicious downloads, or exploiting software vulnerabilities.

Once a device is infected and becomes part of a botnet, it can be used to carry out various malicious activities, including:

  1. Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) Attacks: Botnets can be used to launch DDoS attacks, where a large number of bots simultaneously flood a target website or network with traffic, overwhelming its resources and causing it to become inaccessible.
  2. Spamming and Phishing: Botnets can be used to send out massive volumes of spam emails or phishing messages, often promoting scams, spreading malware, or attempting to steal sensitive information.
  3. Credential Stuffing and Brute-Force Attacks: Botnets can be used to automate credential stuffing attacks, where stolen usernames and passwords are systematically tested against various online services to gain unauthorized access. They can also be used to launch brute-force attacks, attempting to guess passwords through automated trial-and-error methods.
  4. Click Fraud and Ad Fraud: Botnets can be used to generate fraudulent clicks on online advertisements or manipulate online traffic, leading to financial losses for advertisers or artificially inflating website traffic statistics.
  5. Data Theft and Espionage: Botnets can be used to steal sensitive information, such as login credentials, financial data, or intellectual property, from infected devices or networks.
