(a) State the difference between passive and active attacks? What is Cryptographically  generated address? How do they enhance the security

The difference between passive and active attacks:

  1. Passive Attacks: In a passive attack, the attacker intercepts and monitors data without altering it. The goal is to gather information covertly. Passive attacks include activities like eavesdropping on network traffic, capturing sensitive data, or monitoring communication patterns. The attacker does not actively disrupt or modify the targeted system or data.
  2. Active Attacks: In an active attack, the attacker actively modifies or disrupts data or systems. The goal is to directly affect the integrity or availability of the targeted system. Active attacks involve actions such as injecting malicious code, altering data, launching denial-of-service attacks, or gaining unauthorized access to systems.

A cryptographically generated address (CGA) is an address generated using cryptographic techniques. It is commonly used in network protocols, such as IPv6, to enhance security. CGAs provide a way to verify the authenticity and integrity of network addresses.

CGAs enhance security in the following ways:

  1. Address Ownership Verification: CGAs use cryptographic algorithms to generate a unique address that can be linked to a specific entity. This helps verify the ownership of the address and prevents unauthorized use or spoofing.
  2. Address Integrity: CGAs include a digital signature that ensures the integrity of the address. Any modification or tampering with the address can be detected through the verification of the digital signature.
  3. Protection against Address Spoofing: CGAs make it difficult for attackers to impersonate legitimate addresses. The cryptographic techniques used in CGAs make it computationally expensive and time-consuming for attackers to generate valid addresses.

(b) What is social engineering? Explain any three examples each of human based and computer based social engineering.

Social engineering refers to the manipulation of individuals to gain unauthorized access to information or systems. It exploits human psychology, trust, and vulnerabilities to deceive people into revealing sensitive information or performing actions that benefit the attacker.

Three examples of human-based social engineering:

  1. Pretexting: In pretexting, the attacker creates a fictional scenario or pretext to trick individuals into disclosing sensitive information. For example, an attacker might pose as a bank representative and call a person, claiming there is an issue with their account and requesting their login credentials.
  2. Phishing: Phishing is a common form of social engineering where attackers send deceptive emails or messages that appear to be from a legitimate source. The messages often contain links to fake websites that trick users into entering their personal information, such as passwords or credit card details.
  3. Tailgating: Tailgating involves an attacker following an authorized person into a restricted area by pretending to be an employee or someone with legitimate access. The attacker takes advantage of the person's politeness or lack of suspicion to gain physical access to secure areas.

Three examples of computer-based social engineering:

  1. Malware Attacks: Attackers use various techniques, such as email attachments or malicious downloads, to trick users into installing malware on their computers. The malware can then be used to steal sensitive information or gain unauthorized access to systems.
  2. Baiting: Baiting involves enticing users with an offer or reward, such as a free software download or a USB drive left in a public place. Once the user interacts with the bait, malware is installed on their system, allowing the attacker to gain access or control.
  3. Pharming: Pharming is a technique where attackers redirect users to fake websites by manipulating the DNS (Domain Name System) settings or compromising routers. Users unknowingly enter their login credentials or sensitive information on these fake websites, which are then captured by the attacker.
