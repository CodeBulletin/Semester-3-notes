Passive attacks and active attacks are two different types of cyber attacks:
  1. Passive Attacks: In a passive attack, the attacker intercepts and monitors data without altering it. The goal is to gather information covertly. For example, eavesdropping on network traffic to capture sensitive data, such as passwords or credit card numbers, without the knowledge of the users involved.
  2. Active Attacks: Active attacks involve unauthorized modification or destruction of data, or disruption of network services. Unlike passive attacks, active attacks directly affect the integrity or availability of the targeted system. For instance, a hacker might inject malicious code into a website to deface it or launch a denial-of-service attack to make a website inaccessible.

(b) Elaborate Bluetooth hacking. Discuss four common attacks that have emerged in hacking Bluetooth.

Bluetooth hacking refers to unauthorized access or manipulation of Bluetooth-enabled devices. Here are four common attacks that have emerged in Bluetooth hacking:

  1. Bluejacking: Bluejacking involves sending unsolicited messages or business cards to Bluetooth-enabled devices within range. The attacker exploits the device's Bluetooth feature to send messages without pairing or gaining access to the target device. This attack is usually harmless and used for pranks or advertising purposes.
  2. Bluesnarfing: Bluesnarfing is a more serious attack where an attacker gains unauthorized access to a Bluetooth-enabled device to steal or manipulate data. By exploiting vulnerabilities in the Bluetooth protocol, the attacker can access contacts, messages, emails, or other sensitive information stored on the device.
  3. Bluebugging: Bluebugging is an advanced attack that allows an attacker to take control of a Bluetooth-enabled device. Once compromised, the attacker can make calls, send messages, access data, or even use the device as a listening device without the user's knowledge.
  4. Denial-of-Service (DoS): Bluetooth DoS attacks aim to disrupt the normal functioning of Bluetooth devices or networks. By flooding the target device or network with excessive connection requests or malicious data, the attacker can render the Bluetooth service unavailable, causing inconvenience or financial losses.
