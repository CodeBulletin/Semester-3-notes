(a) What is cyberterrorism? Compare cyber espionage and cyberwarfare

  1. Cyberterrorism refers to the use of cyber attacks by individuals or groups to create fear, panic, or disruption for political, ideological, or religious purposes. It involves the deliberate targeting of critical infrastructure, government systems, or public networks to cause damage, disrupt services, or spread fear among the population.
  2. Cyber espionage, on the other hand, involves unauthorized access to computer systems or networks to gather sensitive information for political, economic, or military purposes. It is often conducted by nation-states or state-sponsored actors to gain an advantage in areas such as intelligence, defense, or economic competition.
  3. Cyberwarfare refers to the use of cyber attacks as a means of waging war or conducting military operations. It involves the use of cyber weapons and tactics to disrupt or disable enemy systems, gather intelligence, or launch offensive operations. Unlike cyberterrorism, cyberwarfare is typically conducted by nation-states or state-sponsored entities in the context of armed conflicts.

While cyberterrorism, cyber espionage, and cyberwarfare share some similarities in terms of using cyber attacks, their motivations, targets, and objectives differ significantly..

(c) Contrast spam with spoofing. Enlist two methods to control spamming.

Spam refers to unsolicited and unwanted bulk emails sent to a large number of recipients, typically for advertising or fraudulent purposes. Spoofing, on the other hand, is a technique used to falsify the source of an email or network communication to make it appear as if it is coming from a different sender or source.

Contrast between spam and spoofing:

  1. Spam: Spam emails are unwanted and unsolicited messages sent in bulk to a large number of recipients. They often contain advertisements, scams, or malicious content. The primary goal of spam is to reach as many recipients as possible, often without their consent, to promote products, services, or fraudulent schemes.
  2. Spoofing: Spoofing involves manipulating the source information of an email or network communication to make it appear as if it is coming from a different sender or source. This can include falsifying the sender's email address, IP address, or other identifying information. Spoofing can be used for various purposes, such as phishing attacks, email scams, or hiding the true origin of an attack.

Methods to control spamming:

  1. Email Filters: Implementing email filters or spam filters can help automatically detect and block spam emails based on predefined rules, patterns, or blacklists. These filters analyze various aspects of the email, such as sender information, content, and attachments, to determine if it is spam.
  2. User Education and Awareness: Educating users about safe email practices, recognizing spam emails, and avoiding clicking on suspicious links or downloading attachments from unknown sources can help reduce the risk of falling victim to spam or phishing attacks.

(d) Compare ethical hackers with crackers. List any 3 rules to be abided by the ethical hackers.

Ethical hackers, also known as white hat hackers or security researchers, are individuals who use their technical skills and knowledge to identify vulnerabilities and weaknesses in computer systems, networks, or applications. Their goal is to help organizations improve their security by identifying and fixing potential vulnerabilities before malicious hackers can exploit them.

Crackers, on the other hand, are individuals who engage in hacking activities with malicious intent. They exploit vulnerabilities, gain unauthorized access, steal data, or cause damage to computer systems or networks.

Comparison between ethical hackers and crackers:

  1. Intent: Ethical hackers have a legitimate and lawful intent to identify and fix vulnerabilities, whereas crackers have malicious intent to exploit vulnerabilities for personal gain or to cause harm.
  2. Authorization: Ethical hackers perform their activities with proper authorization from the system owners, whereas crackers engage in unauthorized activities.
  3. Ethics: Ethical hackers adhere to a code of ethics, which includes respecting privacy, confidentiality, and legal boundaries. Crackers disregard ethical considerations and engage in illegal activities.

Three rules to be abided by ethical hackers:

  1. Obtain Proper Authorization: Ethical hackers should always obtain written permission from the system owner or authorized personnel before conducting any security testing or vulnerability assessments.
  2. Maintain Confidentiality: Ethical hackers must respect the confidentiality of any sensitive information they come across during their assessments. They should not disclose or misuse any confidential data they encounter.
  3. Report Findings Responsibly: Ethical hackers should report their findings and vulnerabilities to the system owner or appropriate authorities promptly and responsibly. They should provide clear and detailed information to help the organization address and fix the identified vulnerabilities.

(e) What is role of polymorphic generators in polymorphic virus?

Polymorphic generators play a crucial role in polymorphic viruses. Polymorphic viruses are a type of malware that can change their code or appearance each time they replicate, making it difficult for traditional antivirus software to detect and block them.

Polymorphic generators are algorithms or routines within the virus code that are responsible for generating new, unique variants of the virus. These generators use various techniques, such as encryption, obfuscation, or code mutation, to create different versions of the virus with each replication. By constantly changing their code, polymorphic viruses can evade signature-based detection methods used by antivirus software.

The role of polymorphic generators is to ensure that each new variant of the virus is sufficiently different from previous versions, making it challenging for antivirus software to recognize and block them. This allows the polymorphic virus to continue spreading and infecting systems without being easily detected.

(f) Justify botnets can be used as a fuel in cybercrime.

Botnets, which are networks of compromised computers controlled by a central command-and-control (C&C) server, can be used as a fuel in cybercrime due to their capabilities and characteristics. Here are some justifications:

  1. Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) Attacks: Botnets can be used to launch large-scale DDoS attacks, where multiple compromised computers simultaneously flood a target system or network with a massive amount of traffic, causing it to become overwhelmed and unavailable to legitimate users. Cybercriminals can rent or utilize botnets to extort money from targeted organizations or disrupt their operations.
  2. Spam Distribution: Botnets can be utilized to distribute spam emails on a massive scale. By leveraging the computational power and network bandwidth of the compromised computers, cybercriminals can send out a high volume of spam messages, promoting various scams, phishing attempts, or malware distribution.
  3. Credential Theft and Fraud: Botnets can be used to steal sensitive information, such as login credentials or financial data, from compromised computers. This stolen information can be used for identity theft, financial fraud, or unauthorized access to online accounts.
  4. Malware Distribution: Botnets can serve as a distribution platform for malware. Cybercriminals can use the compromised computers to host and distribute malicious software, such as ransomware, spyware, or banking trojans, to infect more systems and expand their criminal operations.

(g) Elaborate steganography. Contrast steganography with cryptography.

Steganography is the practice of concealing secret or sensitive information within seemingly innocuous or unrelated data or media, such as images, audio files, or documents. It involves embedding the hidden information in a way that is not easily detectable by casual observers.

Contrast between steganography and cryptography:

  • Steganography: Steganography focuses on hiding the existence of the secret information by embedding it within other data. The goal is to make the presence of the hidden information undetectable. Steganography does not necessarily involve encryption or altering the original data.
  • Cryptography: Cryptography involves transforming the original data or information using mathematical algorithms to make it unintelligible to unauthorized individuals. The goal of cryptography is to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity of the data. Cryptographic techniques often involve encryption and decryption processes.

While both steganography and cryptography aim to protect sensitive information, they differ in their approach. Steganography focuses on hiding the information, while cryptography focuses on transforming the information to make it secure.

(h) How do we realize that our system is infected by malware?

Indications that a system may be infected by malware can vary depending on the specific malware and its behavior. However, some common signs that may indicate a malware infection include:

  1. Slow Performance: If your system suddenly becomes significantly slower than usual, it could be a sign of malware activity. Malware can consume system resources, causing sluggishness and delays in normal operations.
  2. Unexpected Pop-ups or Advertisements: If you start seeing an excessive number of pop-up windows or intrusive advertisements, especially when you are not browsing the internet, it could indicate the presence of adware or potentially unwanted programs (PUPs).
  3. Unusual Network Activity: If your network activity is unusually high, even when you are not actively using the internet, it could be a sign of malware. Malware may communicate with remote servers or participate in botnet activities, leading to increased network traffic.
  4. Unauthorized System Changes: If you notice unauthorized changes to your system settings, such as new browser toolbars, unfamiliar icons, or modified browser homepage, it could indicate the presence of malware that has altered your system configuration.
  5. Disabled Security Software: If your antivirus or security software suddenly becomes disabled or stops functioning properly without any action from your side, it could be a sign of malware attempting to evade detection.

(i) What is cryptographically generated address? How do they enhance the security? 

A cryptographically generated address (CGA) is an IPv6 address that is generated using cryptographic techniques to enhance its security and authenticity. CGAs are used to verify the identity of a device or host in an IPv6 network.

CGAs are created by combining the device's public key with other parameters, such as the device's IPv6 address and a nonce. This combination is then hashed to generate a unique CGA. The resulting CGA serves as a digital signature that can be used to verify the authenticity and integrity of the device's communication in the network.

CGAs enhance security by providing a means to verify that the device claiming a particular IPv6 address is the legitimate owner of that address. This helps prevent address spoofing or unauthorized use of IP addresses in the network. By using CGAs, network administrators can have increased confidence in the identity and trustworthiness of devices communicating over IPv6.

(j) Compare and contrast trade name and trade secret.

Trade Name: A trade name, also known as a business name or a company name, is the name under which a business operates or conducts its commercial activities. It is used to identify and distinguish a business from others in the marketplace. A trade name is protected under trademark law and provides exclusive rights to the business to use that name in its specific industry or geographic region.

Example: Coca-Cola is a well-known trade name that identifies the brand and products of The Coca-Cola Company.

Trade Secret: A trade secret refers to confidential and valuable business information that provides a competitive advantage to a company. It can include formulas, processes, customer lists, marketing strategies, or any other confidential information that is not generally known or easily accessible to others. Unlike patents or trademarks, trade secrets are not publicly registered or disclosed. Instead, they are protected through confidentiality agreements, non-disclosure agreements, and other contractual arrangements.

Example: The secret recipe of Coca-Cola is a famous trade secret that has been closely guarded by The Coca-Cola Company for decades.

While trade names focus on the identification and branding of a business, trade secrets protect valuable confidential information that gives a competitive edge to a company.
