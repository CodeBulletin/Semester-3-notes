(a) Explain the following term providing an example
1. Modus Ponen
2. Modus Tollen

Modus Ponens (also known as "affirming the antecedent"):

  • Structure: If P, then Q. P is true. Therefore, Q is true.
  • Example: If it rains, the ground will be wet. It is raining. Therefore, the ground is wet.

Modus Tollens (also known as "denying the consequent"):

  • Structure: If P, then Q. Q is not true. Therefore, P is not true.
  • Example: If it is a cat, it has four legs. My pet does not have four legs. Therefore, my pet is not a cat.

In both cases, the structure of the argument is crucial to its validity. Modus Ponens affirms the first statement to reach a conclusion, while Modus Tollens negates the second statement to reach a different conclusion. Both are widely used in mathematical and philosophical reasoning.

(b) Summarize approaches to Knowledge Representation (KR).

Knowledge representation is a crucial component of artificial intelligence and cognitive science. It involves the encoding of information in a form that can be used to reason, make decisions, and solve problems. Two common approaches to knowledge representation are Propositional Logic and First-Order Predicate Logic.

Propositional Logic: Propositional logic, also known as propositional calculus, is a simple form of logic that deals with propositions or statements. In this approach, knowledge is represented using atomic propositions and logical operators to connect them. Here are the key components of propositional logic:

  1. Atomic Propositions: These are basic statements that are either true or false. They represent facts or conditions in the world.
  2. Logical Operators: Propositional logic uses operators such as AND (∧), OR (∨), NOT (¬), IMPLIES (→), and IF AND ONLY IF (↔) to combine and manipulate propositions.
  3. Truth Tables: Truth tables are used to represent the truth values of compound propositions based on the truth values of their constituent atomic propositions.

First-Order Predicate Logic: First-order predicate logic, also known as first-order logic or FOL, is a more expressive and versatile logic system for knowledge representation. It extends propositional logic by introducing variables, quantifiers, and predicates. Here are the key components of first-order predicate logic:

  1. Constants and Variables: Constants represent specific objects or individuals, while variables can stand for any object within a specified domain.
  2. Predicates: Predicates represent relationships or properties of objects. They take arguments (constants, variables) and can be used to make statements about the world.
  3. Quantifiers: First-order logic uses quantifiers, such as ∀ (universal quantifier) and ∃ (existential quantifier), to express statements about all or some objects within a domain.
  4. Functions: Functions can be used to represent operations or relationships that produce new objects based on existing objects.
  5. Connectives: Logical connectives like ∧ (AND), ∨ (OR), ¬ (NOT), → (IMPLIES), and ↔ (IF AND ONLY IF) are also used in first-order logic to build complex statements.
