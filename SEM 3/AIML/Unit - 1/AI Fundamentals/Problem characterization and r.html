Problem characterization and reduction

Problem Characterization:

  • Problem characterization involves gaining a deep understanding of a complex problem by examining its various aspects, constraints, and requirements. This step is crucial before attempting to solve the problem.
  • Key aspects of problem characterization include:
    • Defining the problem: Clearly articulating the problem statement and its objectives.
    • Identifying constraints: Recognizing any limitations, restrictions, or conditions that must be adhered to during problem-solving.
    • Gathering data: Collecting relevant information and data that can inform the problem-solving process.
    • Understanding stakeholders: Identifying the individuals or entities affected by or interested in the problem and their perspectives.
  • Problem characterization helps in framing the problem correctly, which is essential for selecting the most appropriate problem-solving approach.

Problem Reduction:

  • Problem reduction is a strategy used to simplify complex problems by breaking them down into smaller, more manageable subproblems or components. Each subproblem is easier to solve than the original problem.
  • The process of problem reduction involves:
    • Decomposition: Dividing the problem into smaller, interconnected parts or subproblems.
    • Abstraction: Focusing on the essential elements of each subproblem while temporarily ignoring irrelevant details.
    • Solving subproblems: Addressing each subproblem individually, often using specialized techniques or algorithms.
    • Integration: Combining the solutions to the subproblems to arrive at a solution for the original, larger problem.
  • Problem reduction can make complex problems more tractable, as it allows for the application of specific problem-solving methods to each subproblem, potentially leading to more efficient solutions.
