Universal Quantifier

In first-order predicate logic, the universal quantifier (∀) expresses that a statement applies to all elements of a specified domain. It's symbolized by ∀ and read as "for all," "for every," or "for each."

Key Points:

  • Notation: Written as ∀x P(x), meaning "for every x, P(x) is true."
  • Meaning: Asserts that P(x) is true for every element x in the domain of discourse, the set of all considered objects.
  • Scope: The variable x is bound within the expression, focusing on the universal truth of property P across all x.
  • Examples:
    • In mathematics: ∀x (x² ≥ 0) means "for all x, x squared is greater than or equal to 0."
    • In everyday language: ∀x (If x is a bird, then x has wings) translates to "All birds have wings."
  • Contrast with Existential Quantifier: Unlike the existential quantifier (∃), the universal quantifier applies to all elements, not just any one.
  • Use in Proofs: Crucial in formal logic and mathematics for general statements about sets or categories.

Understanding the universal quantifier is key in formal logic for expressing broad principles and theorems.
