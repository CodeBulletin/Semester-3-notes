Non - Monotonic

Non-monotonic reasoning is a type of reasoning in artificial intelligence and logic that allows for the revision of conclusions based on new information without invalidating previously drawn conclusions. In non-monotonic reasoning, unlike classical logic, adding additional facts or rules may lead to the retraction or revision of prior inferences. This approach is essential for handling uncertainty and incomplete information in AI systems.

Key Characteristics of Non-Monotonic Reasoning:

  1. Default Reasoning: Non-monotonic reasoning often involves default rules or assumptions that can be overridden or revised in the presence of new evidence. These defaults represent tentative conclusions that are assumed to be true unless contradicted.
  2. Closed-World Assumption: In non-monotonic reasoning, there is a closed-world assumption, meaning that if a statement is not explicitly known to be true, it is considered false. However, this assumption can be overridden by default rules.
  3. Conflict Resolution: When conflicts arise between default rules or conclusions, non-monotonic reasoning mechanisms attempt to resolve these conflicts by taking into account the specificity or reliability of the information.

Example of Non-Monotonic Reasoning:

Consider a non-monotonic reasoning scenario involving a bird classification system. We have the following default rules:

  1. If an animal has feathers, it is classified as a bird (default assumption).
  2. If an animal can fly, it is classified as a bird (default assumption).
  3. Penguins have feathers, but they cannot fly (specific information).

Now, let's apply non-monotonic reasoning to classify animals based on these rules:

  • Given a generic animal X with feathers, non-monotonic reasoning would initially classify X as a bird based on the first rule (default assumption).
  • However, if we encounter specific information about a penguin, which has feathers but cannot fly (contradicting the second rule), non-monotonic reasoning revises the classification for penguins. It recognizes that the initial assumption based on the second rule was incorrect in this specific case.

So, in non-monotonic reasoning, the classification of penguins as birds is revised or overridden when we encounter the specific information that they cannot fly. This illustrates the ability of non-monotonic reasoning to handle exceptions and revise conclusions based on new evidence without violating the default assumptions entirely.
