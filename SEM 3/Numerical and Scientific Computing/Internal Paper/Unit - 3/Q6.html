(b) Derive Taylor Series Theorem

The Taylor series theorem provides a way to approximate a sufficiently smooth function \( f(x) \) near a point \( a \) by a polynomial constructed from the derivatives of \(f\) at  \(a\). Here's a derivation and explanation of the Taylor series:

Function and Its Derivatives

Consider a function \(f(x)\) that has \(n\) continuous derivatives on an interval containing \(a\) and  \(x\).

Taylor Series Expansion

The Taylor series of \(f\) around  \(a\) is given by:

\(f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x - a)^2 + \frac{f'''(a)}{3!}(x - a)^3 + \cdots + \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x - a)^n + R_n(x)\)

where \(f^{(n)}(a)\) is the  \(n^{\th}\) derivative of \(f\) evaluated at \(a\), and \(R_n(x)\) is the remainder term, representing the error in the approximation.

Derivation of the Formula

1. Start with \(n=0\): The simplest case, \(f(x) \approx f(a)\), is a constant approximation (zeroth derivative).

2. First Derivative (Linear approximation): Consider the tangent line to \(f\) at  \(a\). The equation of the tangent line is \(f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)\).

3. Higher Derivatives: To improve the approximation, include terms involving higher derivatives. By considering the rate of change of the function and its derivatives at \(a\), we construct the polynomial approximation.

4. General Term: The general term of the Taylor series is derived from the \(n^{\th}\) derivative of \(f\) at \(a\), leading to the term \(\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x - a)^n \).

5. Infinite Series: By extending this process to an infinite number of derivatives, we get the full Taylor series.

Remainder Term (Error)

The remainder term \(R_n(x)\) in the Taylor series is crucial for understanding the accuracy of the approximation. One common form of the remainder term is given by the Lagrange form:

\(R_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!}(x - a)^{(n+1)}\)

for some \(\xi\) between  \(a\) and \(x\). This term gives the error between the actual value of the function and its Taylor approximation up to the \(n^{\th}\) term.

Conclusion

The Taylor series provides a powerful tool in analysis and applied mathematics for approximating functions using polynomials. It is especially useful when dealing with complex functions where direct evaluation is difficult. The series shows how a function can be expressed as an infinite sum of terms calculated from its derivatives at a single point.
