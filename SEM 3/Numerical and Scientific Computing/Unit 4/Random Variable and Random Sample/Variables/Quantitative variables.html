Quantitative variables

Quantitative variables, also known as numerical variables, are variables that represent quantifiable quantitiesâ€”meaning they are measured in numbers and can be used in arithmetic operations. These variables are essential in statistical analysis and scientific research because they provide a means for measuring, calculating, and comparing quantities. Quantitative variables are generally divided into two categories:

Continuous Variables:

  • Continuous variables can take on any value within a given range. The range might be finite or infinite.
  • They are not restricted to discrete, separate values, but rather can occupy any value on a continuum within a range.
  • Examples include:
    • Height: People can be 170.5 cm, 170.51 cm, 170.52 cm, etc.
    • Time: It can be measured to any level of precision, like seconds, milliseconds, or even smaller units.
    • Temperature: It can vary continuously and can be measured to several decimal places.
  • Continuous variables are often measured, rather than counted.

Discrete Variables:

  • Discrete variables consist of countable values. They can take on a finite or countably infinite number of values.
  • These values are distinct and separate, which means the variable cannot take on values between these distinct points.
  • Examples include:
    • Number of children in a family: You can have 1, 2, 3 children, etc., but not 2.5 children.
    • Number of cars in a parking lot.
    • Number of questions answered correctly on a test.
  • Discrete variables are typically counted, not measured.

Quantitative variables are fundamental in various types of statistical analyses. They allow for a range of mathematical and statistical operations, including calculating averages, variances, and correlations. They are used to perform various types of statistical tests and are key in the creation of graphs and charts like histograms, scatter plots, and line graphs.

In research and data analysis, understanding whether a variable is discrete or continuous helps determine the appropriate analytical approach and the type of statistical tests that can be applied. For example, certain parametric tests are suitable for continuous data, while non-parametric tests may be more appropriate for discrete data.
