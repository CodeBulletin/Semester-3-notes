Random Variables

Random variables are a fundamental concept in statistics and probability theory. They are used to quantify the outcomes of random phenomena. There are two main types of random variables:

Discrete Random Variables:

  • These are variables that can take on a countable number of distinct values.
  • Common examples include the roll of a die (which can result in 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6), the number of heads in a series of coin tosses, or the number of students present in a classroom.
  • The probability distribution of a discrete random variable is often represented by a probability mass function (PMF), which gives the probability of each possible value.

Continuous Random Variables:

  • These variables can take on an infinite number of possible values, often within a certain range.
  • Examples include the measurement of time it takes for something to happen, the height of students in a school, or the temperature on a given day.
  • The probability distribution of a continuous random variable is described by a probability density function (PDF), which describes the relative likelihood of any outcome within a range of values.

Each type of random variable has its own methods and formulas for calculating probabilities and expectations, reflecting the different nature of discrete and continuous outcomes.
