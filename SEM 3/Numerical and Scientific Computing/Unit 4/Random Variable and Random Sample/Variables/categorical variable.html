categorical variable

A categorical variable, also known as a qualitative variable, is a type of variable that can take on one of a limited, and usually fixed, number of possible values, assigning each individual or other unit of observation to a particular group or nominal category on the basis of some qualitative property. In contrast to numeric variables (like height or age), categorical variables are often non-numerical or have numerical values that donâ€™t have a mathematical meaning.

Categorical variables are commonly divided into two types:

Nominal Variables:

  • These variables have two or more categories without any kind of order or priority.
  • Examples include gender (male, female), blood type (A, B, AB, O), and marital status (single, married, divorced, widowed).
  • The categories are distinct and there is no inherent order to the categories.

Ordinal Variables:

  • These variables have two or more categories just like nominal variables but the categories have an inherent order.
  • An example is a rating scale of 1 to 5 that measures satisfaction or pain, where each number corresponds to a level of satisfaction or pain intensity.
  • The order of the categories is meaningful, but the differences between the categories are not necessarily uniform (i.e., the difference between categories 1 and 2 may not be the same as between categories 3 and 4).

In data analysis, it's important to identify categorical variables because they require different handling than numeric variables. For example, most statistical models require that categorical variables be encoded in a way that is suitable for the model. This often involves creating dummy variables or using techniques like one-hot encoding to transform the categories into a format that can be used in mathematical calculations.
