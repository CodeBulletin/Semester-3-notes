Method of least squares

The method of least squares is a mathematical approach used in regression analysis and curve fitting to find the best-fitting line (or curve) through a set of data points. The goal is to minimize the sum of the squares of the vertical distances (residuals) between the observed data points and the points predicted by the model.

Here's a simplified explanation of how the method of least squares works, focusing on linear regression:

Define the Model:

  • In linear regression, the model is typically represented as a straight line: \(y = mx + b\), where  \(y\) is the dependent variable, \(x\) is the independent variable, \(m\) is the slope of the line, and \(b\) is the y-intercept.

Calculate Residuals:

  • For each data point, calculate the vertical distance (residual) between the observed \(y\)-value and the  \(y\)-value predicted by the model. The residual is the difference between the observed and predicted values.

Square the Residuals:

  • Square each of the residuals obtained in step 2. Squaring is done to ensure that the positive and negative residuals do not cancel each other out when summing them up.

Minimize the Sum of Squared Residuals:

  • Find the values of \(m\) and  \(b\) that minimize the sum of the squared residuals. This is often done by taking the partial derivatives of the sum of squared residuals with respect to \(m\) and  \(b\), setting them equal to zero, and solving the resulting system of equations.

Coefficients and Best-Fitting Line:

  • The values of \(m\) and  \(b\) obtained in step 4 are the coefficients of the best-fitting line. This line represents the relationship between the variables that minimizes the overall squared differences between observed and predicted values.
