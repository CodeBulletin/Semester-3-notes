Parameter Estimation

Point Estimators:

  • A point estimator is a single value that is used to estimate the unknown parameter of a population.
  • It provides a specific estimate or best guess for the parameter.
  • Common examples of point estimators include the sample mean, sample median, and sample proportion.
  • The point estimator is denoted by a specific value, such as  \(\hat \theta\), where \(\theta\) represents the parameter of interest.

Interval Estimators:

  • An interval estimator, on the other hand, provides a range of values within which the true parameter is believed to lie, along with a level of confidence.
  • It recognizes the uncertainty in estimation by providing a range instead of a single point.
  • Confidence intervals are a common form of interval estimator. For example, a 95% confidence interval for the population mean is a range of values within which we are 95% confident that the true population mean lies.
  • The interval estimator is denoted by \((\theta_L,\theta_U)\), where \(\theta_L\)​ is the lower bound and \(\theta_U\)​ is the upper bound of the interval.

Comparison:

  • Point estimators are simpler and provide a specific estimate, but they do not convey the uncertainty associated with the estimate.
  • Interval estimators provide a range of values, allowing for a more informative representation of the uncertainty in the estimation process.
  • Interval estimators are often preferred in situations where it's important to communicate the precision of the estimate along with the estimate itself.
  • The choice between point and interval estimation depends on the goals of the analysis and the information needed by decision-makers.
