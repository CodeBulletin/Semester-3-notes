Gauss Seidal

The Gauss-Seidel method is an iterative numerical technique used for solving systems of linear equations. It's often employed when you have a large system of linear equations and you want to find an approximate solution

Gauss-Seidel generally converges faster than Gauss-Jacobi. In Gauss-Jacobi, all the values from the previous iteration are used to calculate the values for the next iteration. In contrast, Gauss-Seidel uses updated values as soon as they become available. This leads to quicker convergence in many cases, which means you need fewer iterations to reach a solution.

Rate of Convergence of Gauss Seidal Method

The rate of convergence of the Gauss-Seidel method depends on the characteristics of the system of linear equations being solved. In particular, the rate of convergence is influenced by the properties of the coefficient matrix and the initial guess for the solution. Here are some key factors that affect the rate of convergence:

  1. Diagonal Dominance: The rate of convergence is typically faster when the coefficient matrix is diagonally dominant. Diagonal dominance means that the absolute value of each diagonal element is greater than the sum of the absolute values of the off-diagonal elements in the same row. When the matrix is diagonally dominant, Gauss-Seidel often converges rapidly.
  2. Spectral Radius: The convergence rate can be quantified using the spectral radius (ρ) of the iteration matrix. The iteration matrix is obtained by dividing each row of the coefficient matrix by its corresponding diagonal element. If ρ is less than 1, the method converges, and the closer ρ is to 1, the slower the convergence. If ρ is very close to 0, convergence is rapid.
  3. Initial Guess: The choice of the initial guess also affects the rate of convergence. A good initial guess that is closer to the actual solution can lead to faster convergence. Conversely, a poor initial guess may result in slow convergence or divergence.
  4. Eigenvalues: The eigenvalues of the iteration matrix can provide insights into the convergence behavior. If all the eigenvalues are close to zero, it indicates slow convergence, while eigenvalues significantly less than 1 suggest faster convergence.
  5. Matrix Conditioning: The condition number of the coefficient matrix can impact convergence. If the matrix is ill-conditioned, meaning it has a high condition number, convergence may be slow. Conditioning issues can result in numerical instability.

In practice, the rate of convergence of Gauss-Seidel is often assessed empirically, and adjustments may be made to improve convergence. For example, relaxation techniques like successive over-relaxation (SOR) can be used to accelerate convergence in some cases.
