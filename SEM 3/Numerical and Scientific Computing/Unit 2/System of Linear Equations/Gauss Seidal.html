Gauss Seidal

The Gauss-Seidel method is an iterative numerical technique used for solving systems of linear equations. It's often employed when you have a large system of linear equations and you want to find an approximate solution

Gauss-Seidel generally converges faster than Gauss-Jacobi. In Gauss-Jacobi, all the values from the previous iteration are used to calculate the values for the next iteration. In contrast, Gauss-Seidel uses updated values as soon as they become available. This leads to quicker convergence in many cases, which means you need fewer iterations to reach a solution.

Rate of Convergence of Gauss Seidal Method

The convergence rate of the Gauss-Seidel method for linear equations depends on the system's characteristics. Key factors include:

  1. Diagonal Dominance: Faster convergence if the matrix is diagonally dominant, where diagonal elements outweigh the sum of off-diagonal elements in each row.
  2. Spectral Radius: Convergence rate relates to the spectral radius (ρ) of the iteration matrix. Lower ρ indicates faster convergence.
  3. Initial Guess: A closer initial guess to the actual solution speeds up convergence, while a poor guess may slow it down or cause divergence.
  4. Eigenvalues: Eigenvalues near zero suggest slow convergence; values significantly less than 1 indicate faster convergence.
  5. Matrix Conditioning: Ill-conditioned matrices (high condition number) may slow convergence.

Convergence is often assessed empirically, with techniques like successive over-relaxation (SOR) sometimes used to enhance it.
