Refinement of solutions

Refinement of solutions in solving systems of linear equations is a technique used to improve the accuracy of a solution obtained by numerical methods. Numerical solutions to linear systems, such as those obtained through methods like LU Decomposition, Gaussian Elimination, or iterative methods, can be imprecise due to rounding errors and other computational limitations. Refinement steps help reduce these errors and provide a more accurate solution. Here's a general approach to refining solutions:

Step 1: Obtain an Initial Solution

First, solve the system using your chosen numerical method to get an initial solution. Let's denote this solution as \(x_0\)

Step 2: Calculate the Residual

The residual vector, \(r\), is the difference between the right-hand side of the equation and the product of the matrix and the initial solution. For a system \(Ax=b\), the residual is calculated as:

\(r=b-Ax_0\)

Step 3: Solve for the Error

Treat the residual as a new right-hand side and solve the system  \(Ae=r\) to find the error term \(e\). You can use the same decomposition (e.g., LU) used to solve the original system.

Step 4: Update the Solution

Adjust your initial solution with the error term:

\(x_1=x_0+e\)

This updated solution \(x_1\)​ is typically more accurate than the initial solution \(x_0\)​.

Step 5: Iterate if Necessary

If the residual is still larger than your desired tolerance, you can repeat the refinement process with \(x_1\) as the new initial solution.

Considerations

  • Tolerance: Before starting, decide on a tolerance level for the accuracy of your solution. This will dictate when you can stop the refinement process.
  • Convergence: Check if the refinement is converging towards a solution. If the residual does not decrease significantly after a few iterations, there may be issues with the system or the numerical method used.
  • Stability: Be aware that not all problems are well-suited for refinement. In some cases, the system of equations might be ill-conditioned, meaning that small errors in data can lead to large errors in the solution.
  • Computational Cost: Each refinement step involves additional computations. Balance the need for accuracy with the available computational resources.

Refinement is particularly useful in engineering and scientific computations where high accuracy is required. It's a powerful tool to enhance the reliability of solutions obtained from numerical methods.
