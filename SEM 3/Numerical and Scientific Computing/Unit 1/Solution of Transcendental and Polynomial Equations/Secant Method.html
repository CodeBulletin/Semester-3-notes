Secant Method

The Secant Method is a numerical technique used for finding approximate solutions to equations, specifically to find the roots of a continuous function. It's an iterative method that refines the estimate of a root with each iteration. The Secant Method is an improvement over the Bisection Method and the Regula Falsi Method in terms of convergence speed, as it can converge faster when the function's behavior is well-behaved.

Here's how the Secant Method works:

Choose two initial points, \(x_0, x_1\) that are reasonably close to the root.

Calculate the function values \(f(x_0)\) and  \(f(x_1)\) at these initial points.

Compute the next approximation to the root using linear interpolation. The formula for this is:

\(x_{n+1} = \frac{x_nf(x_{n-1}) -  x_{n-1}f(x_n)}{f(x_{n-1}) -f(x_{n})}\)

Update the values of \(x_{n-1}\) and  \(x_n\) to \(x_n\) and  \(x_{n+1}\), respectively, for the next iteration.

Repeat steps 2 to 4 iteratively until the desired level of accuracy is achieved.

The Secant Method essentially uses the slope of the secant line connecting the points \((x_{n-1}, f(x_{n-1}))\) and  \((x_{n}, f(x_{n}))\) to estimate where the root might be. This linear interpolation helps the method converge more quickly than linear methods like the Bisection and Regula Falsi methods

Order of Convergence


The order of convergence of the Secant Method is approximately 1.6181.618 or the golden ratio ϕ. The order of convergence indicates how quickly the method converges to the root as the number of iterations increases.

The Secant Method exhibits superlinear convergence, which is faster than linear convergence (as seen in methods like the Bisection or Regula Falsi methods), but generally slower than quadratic convergence (as seen in methods like Newton's Method under certain conditions).

Mathematically, if \(e_n\)​ represents the error in the \(n^{th}\) iteration (the difference between the true root and the approximation obtained after n iterations), the order of convergence p of the Secant Method can be estimated by looking at the limit:

\(p \approx \frac{log(\frac{|e_{n+1}|}{|e_n|})}{log(\frac{|e_{n}|}{|e_{n-1}|})}\)

If the limit above converges to a value close to 1.618 (the golden ratio ϕ), it indicates that the method's order of convergence is superlinear and is around the golden ratio.

This means that in each iteration, the number of accurate digits approximately increases by a factor of 1.6181.618, making the Secant Method relatively faster than linear methods but not as fast as quadratic methods. However, it's important to note that the order of convergence might vary depending on the specific behavior of the function and the choice of initial points.
