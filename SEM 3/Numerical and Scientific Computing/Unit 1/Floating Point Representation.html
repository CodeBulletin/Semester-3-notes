Floating Point Representation

Floating Point Representation: Overview and Challenges

What is Floating Point Representation? Floating-point representation is a computing method for storing real numbers using a finite number of bits. It approximates real numbers by allowing the decimal point to "float," adjusting the exponent to represent a range of values. It comprises:

  • Sign bit: Indicates positive (0) or negative (1) numbers.
  • Exponent: Scales the number, covering a wide magnitude range.
  • Mantissa: Represents precision or the fractional part.

Numbers are stored in scientific notation (base 2), combining the significand and an exponent of 2.

Pitfalls of Floating Point Representation Despite its versatility, floating-point representation has limitations:

  1. Rounding Errors: Inevitable approximations can lead to accumulated errors.
  2. Limited Precision: Finite bits limit precision, especially with extreme values.
  3. Non-Associativity: Order of operations affects results, risking inaccuracies.
  4. Inexact Representations: Some decimals, like 0.1, can't be exactly represented.
  5. Denormalized Numbers: Very small numbers lose accuracy.
  6. Overflow and Underflow: Exceeding exponent ranges causes extremes or zeroes.
  7. NaN and Infinity: Special values need careful handling.
  8. Comparisons: Direct equality checks are unreliable due to rounding errors.
  9. Portability Issues: Variations across hardware and languages affect consistency.
  10. Performance Trade-offs: Floating-point operations are slower than integer ones, affecting performance on limited resources.
