Euler's method

Euler's method is a simple and widely used numerical method for approximating the solution of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). It is named after the Swiss mathematician Leonhard Euler, who introduced it in the 18th century.

Euler's method is based on the idea of approximating the solution by taking small steps along the derivative of the function at each point. The method is derived from the first-order Taylor series expansion.

Here's how Euler's method works:

Start with an initial condition: a known value of the solution at a specific point, often denoted as "x₀" and "y₀" for the independent and dependent variables, respectively.

Choose a step size, denoted as "h," which determines the distance between consecutive points in the approximation.

Use the initial condition to calculate the derivative of the function at the starting point: "f(x₀, y₀)." This derivative represents the slope of the function at that point.

Approximate the value of the solution at the next point, "x₁," by taking a step of size "h" in the direction of the derivative:

y₁ = y₀ + h * f(x₀, y₀)

This equation represents the Euler's method update formula.

Repeat steps 3 and 4, using the newly approximated value as the initial condition, to calculate the solution at subsequent points. Continue this process until the desired range or number of points is reached.

Euler's method is relatively straightforward to implement and computationally efficient. However, it is a first-order method, meaning that the error in the approximation tends to accumulate over time. As a result, it may not provide highly accurate solutions for complex ODEs or when a high level of precision is required.
