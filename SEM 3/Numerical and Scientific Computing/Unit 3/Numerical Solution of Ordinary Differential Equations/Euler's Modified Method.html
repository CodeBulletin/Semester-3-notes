Euler's Modified Method

Euler's Modified Method, also known as the Improved Euler's Method or the Heun's Method, is a modification of the original Euler's method for approximating the solution of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). It is an explicit numerical method that provides a more accurate approximation by considering the average of the slopes at the current and next points.

Here's how Euler's Modified Method works:

Start with an initial condition: a known value of the solution at a specific point, often denoted as "x₀" and "y₀" for the independent and dependent variables, respectively.

Choose a step size, denoted as "h," which determines the distance between consecutive points in the approximation.

Use the initial condition to calculate the derivative of the function at the starting point: "f(x₀, y₀)." This derivative represents the slope of the function at that point.

Approximate the value of the solution at an intermediate point, "x₀ + h/2," using Euler's method:

y_intermediate = y₀ + (h/2) * f(x₀, y₀)

Calculate the derivative of the function at the intermediate point: "f(x₀ + h/2, y_intermediate)."

Use the average of the slopes at the current and next points to update the approximation:

y₁ = y₀ + h * f(x₀ + h/2, y_intermediate)

This equation represents the Euler's Modified Method update formula.

Repeat steps 4 to 6, using the newly approximated value as the initial condition, to calculate the solution at subsequent points. Continue this process until the desired range or number of points is reached.

Euler's Modified Method improves upon the accuracy of the original Euler's method by considering the average slope over the interval between two points. This helps to reduce the error that accumulates in the approximation. However, it is still a first-order method and may not provide highly accurate solutions for complex ODEs or when a high level of precision is required.
