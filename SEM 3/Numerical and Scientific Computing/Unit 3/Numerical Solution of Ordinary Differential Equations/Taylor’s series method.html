Taylor’s series method


Taylor's series method is a numerical technique used to solve ordinary differential equations (ODEs) by approximating the solution as a power series expansion. It is based on Taylor's theorem, which states that a function can be represented as an infinite sum of its derivatives evaluated at a particular point.

The Taylor's series method involves the following steps:

  1. Choose a point (often denoted as "a") around which the Taylor series expansion will be centered.
  2. Assume that the solution to the ODE can be expressed as a power series expansion around the chosen point:
    y(x) = y(a) + (x - a)y'(a) + (x - a)^2/2! y''(a) + (x - a)^3/3! y'''(a) + …
    Here, y(x) represents the unknown solution, y(a) is the value of the solution at the chosen point, and y'(a), y''(a), y'''(a), etc., represent the derivatives of the solution evaluated at the chosen point.
  3. Substitute the power series expansion into the ODE and equate the coefficients of each power of (x - a) to zero. This will result in a system of equations involving the derivatives of the solution evaluated at the chosen point.
  4. Solve the system of equations to determine the values of the derivatives at the chosen point.
  5. Substitute the values of the derivatives back into the power series expansion to obtain an approximation of the solution.

The accuracy of the Taylor's series method depends on the number of terms included in the expansion. Including more terms will result in a more accurate approximation, but it may also increase the computational complexity. In practice, the expansion is often truncated after a certain number of terms to balance accuracy and efficiency.
