AVL Tree

An AVL tree is a self-balancing binary search tree that ensures the tree remains balanced to maintain efficient operations such as searching, insertion, and deletion. The name "AVL" stands for the initials of the inventors, Adelson-Velsky and Landis.

In an AVL tree, each node has a balance factor associated with it, which is the difference between the heights of its left and right subtrees. The balance factor can be either -1, 0, or 1. The tree is considered balanced if the balance factor of every node is in the range of -1 to 1.

Whenever an insertion or deletion operation is performed that may disturb the balance of the tree, AVL trees employ rotation operations to rebalance the tree. Rotations are applied to adjust the heights of the subtrees and maintain the balance factor of each node.

The self-balancing property of AVL trees ensures that the height of the tree remains logarithmic in the number of nodes, resulting in efficient operations with a time complexity of O(log n), where n is the number of elements in the tree.

The balance factor calculation and rotation operations make AVL trees slightly more complex than regular binary search trees, but they provide a guaranteed balanced structure that offers consistent performance for various operations.

An AVL tree has several important properties:

  1. Balanced Structure: In an AVL tree, the heights of the left and right subtrees of any node differ by at most 1. This ensures a balanced structure and guarantees that the tree remains relatively shallow, resulting in efficient operations.
  2. Balance Factor: Each node in an AVL tree stores a balance factor, which is the difference between the height of its left subtree and the height of its right subtree. The balance factor can be -1, 0, or 1. By maintaining balance factors, AVL trees can determine when and where rotations are needed to restore balance.
  3. Self-Balancing Operations: Whenever an insertion or deletion operation is performed that may disrupt the balance of the tree, AVL trees employ rotation operations to restore balance. There are four rotation operations: Left-Left (LL), Right-Right (RR), Left-Right (LR), and Right-Left (RL). These rotations adjust the heights of subtrees and maintain the balance factor of each node.
  4. Efficient Search, Insertion, and Deletion: Due to their balanced nature, AVL trees provide efficient search, insertion, and deletion operations with a time complexity of O(log n), where n is the number of nodes in the tree. This time complexity remains consistent regardless of the distribution of the data.
  5. Unique Key Values: AVL trees are typically used for storing unique key-value pairs, where each key in the tree is unique. If a duplicate key is attempted to be inserted into an AVL tree, it would either be rejected or an appropriate action can be taken based on the specific implementation.
  6. Additional Memory Overhead: AVL trees require extra memory to store the balance factors for each node, in addition to the key and value. This additional memory overhead compared to regular binary search trees is necessary to maintain the self-balancing property.
