Amortized time complexity

Amortized time complexity is a concept in computer science for analyzing an algorithm's average time per operation over a series of operations. It's useful for algorithms with occasional costly operations amid mostly faster ones, as it spreads the cost of expensive operations across the entire sequence, giving a more accurate average performance measure.

Three methods to calculate amortized time complexity are:

  1. Aggregate Analysis: Totals the cost of a series of operations and divides by the number of operations. It works well when operations are similar and their costs can be easily summed.
  2. The Accounting Method: Assigns different costs to operations, with some overpaying to cover future expensive operations, akin to saving money for future costs.
  3. The Potential Method: Similar to the concept of potential energy in physics, it assigns a potential to the data structure after each operation. The potential represents stored work that can be used for future operations. The amortized cost includes the actual cost plus the change in potential.

Amortized time complexity is particularly relevant for data structures like binary heaps, hash tables, and splay trees, where infrequent but costly operations don't significantly affect overall performance.
