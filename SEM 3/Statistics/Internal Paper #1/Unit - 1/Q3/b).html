The Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) and the Standard & Poor's 500 Index (S&P 500) are both used to measure the performance of the stock market. The DJIA is based on the price of stocks for 30 large companies; the S&P 500 is based on the price of stocks for 500 companies. If both the DJIA and S&P 500 measure the performance of the stock market, how are they correlated? The following data show the daily percent increase or daily percent decrease in the DJIA and S&P 500 for a sample of nine days over a three-month period.

(i) Compute the sample correlation coefficient for these data.

\(\sum x = 4.4\)
\(\bar x = 0.49\)
 

\(\sum y = 3.97\)
\(\bar y = 0.44\)

r = 0.747

(ii) Discuss the association between the DJIA and S&P 500. Do you need to check both before having a general idea about the daily stock market performance?

The strong positive correlation (approximately 0.747) between the DJIA (Dow Jones Industrial Average) and the S&P 500 (Standard & Poor's 500 Index) indicates a significant association in their daily performance. This correlation suggests that movements in one index are often mirrored by similar movements in the other.

In short, no, you do not necessarily need to check both the DJIA and the S&P 500 to get a general idea about the daily stock market performance, as there is a strong positive correlation between them. However, for a more comprehensive understanding, considering both can provide a fuller picture due to their differences in composition and coverage.
