a)

A seven-year medical research study reported that women whose mothers took the drug DES during pregnancy were twice as likely to develop tissue abnormalities that might lead to cancer as were women whose mothers did not take the drug.

(I) This study compared two populations. What were the populations?

The study compared two distinct populations:

  1. Women whose mothers took the drug DES during pregnancy.
  2. Women whose mothers did not take the drug DES during pregnancy.

(ii) Do you suppose the data were obtained in a survey or an experiment?

Given the context, it's more likely that the data were obtained through a survey or observational study rather than an experiment. This is because:

  • It involves historical data (drug intake during pregnancy of participants' mothers).
  • An experiment would require controlled administration of the drug, which is not ethical or feasible in this scenario.

(iii) For the population of women whose mothers took the drug DES during pregnancy, a sample of 3980 women showed that 63 developed tissue abnormalities that might lead to cancer. Provide a descriptive statistic that could be used to estimate the number of women out of 1000 in this population who have tissue abnormalities.

For the population of women whose mothers took DES, a sample of 3980 women showed that 63 developed tissue abnormalities. To estimate the number per 1000:
\(Estimated \space number \space per \space 1000=\frac{63}{3980}*1000 = 15.83\)

(iv) For the population of women whose mothers did not take the drug DES during pregnancy, what is the estimate of the number of women out of 1000 who would be expected to have tissue
abnormalities?

\(Estimated \space number \space per \space NONDES \space group=\frac{15.83}{2} = 7.91\)

(v) Medical studies often use a relatively large sample (in this case, 3980). Why?

Large sample sizes, like 3980 in this case, are used in medical studies for several reasons:

  • Increase Statistical Power: Larger samples reduce the margin of error and increase the confidence in the results.
  • Generalizability: A larger sample size is more likely to be representative of the population, making the findings more generalizable.
  • Detect Small Effect Sizes: Larger samples can detect smaller differences or effects that might be missed in smaller samples.
  • Account for Variability: They help account for individual differences and variability in responses to treatments or exposures.
