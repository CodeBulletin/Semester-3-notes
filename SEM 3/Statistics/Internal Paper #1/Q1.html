(b) What do you mean by sample space and events? Explain with an example.

Sample Space: The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. It includes every outcome that could occur, and each outcome is a distinct element of the sample space. The sample space is denoted by the symbol "\(S\)"

Example: Consider rolling a six-sided die. The sample space, in this case, would be \(S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\), as these are all the possible outcomes when rolling the die.

Events: An event is a subset of the sample space, representing a specific outcome or a combination of outcomes. Events can be simple (a single outcome) or compound (multiple outcomes). Events are denoted by capital letters (e.g., A, B, C).

Example: Let's define two events for the die roll example:

  • Event A: Rolling an \(even\) number. In terms of the sample space, \(A = \{2, 4, 6\}\).
  • Event B: Rolling a number greater than \(5\). In terms of the sample space, \(B = \{6\}\).

In this case:

  • A is a compound event because it consists of multiple outcomes.
  • B is a simple event because it represents a single outcome.

Events help us describe and analyze the outcomes of interest in a given probability experiment.

(d) Explain Poisson probability distribution.

The Poisson probability distribution is a discrete probability distribution that describes the number of events that occur within a fixed interval of time or space. It is named after the French mathematician Siméon Denis Poisson, who introduced it in the early 19th century. The key characteristics of the Poisson distribution are:

Discreteness: The Poisson distribution deals with countable events, such as the number of phone calls received at a call center in an hour or the number of arrivals at a service point in a given time period.

Independence: It assumes that the occurrence of one event does not affect the occurrence of another within the specified interval. In other words, events are considered to be independent.

Fixed Rate: The average rate at which events occur is constant over time or space.

The probability mass function (PMF) of the Poisson distribution is given by:

\(P(X = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}*\lambda^k}{k!}\)

Where:

  • \(P(X = k)\) is the probability of observing exactly \(k\) events,
  • \(e\) is the mathematical constant approximately equal to 2.71828,
  • \(\lambda\) is the average rate of event occurrences per interval,
  • \(k\) is the actual number of events observed.

(g) Explain the Chebyshev's theorem.

Chebyshev's Theorem states that for any data distribution (regardless of its shape):

At least \(1 - \frac1{k^2}\) of the data lies within \(k\) standard deviations of the mean, where \(k\) is any positive constant greater than 1.

Mathematically this can be expressed as

\(P(|X - \mu| \le k\sigma) \ge 1 - \frac1{k^2}\)

Where:

  • \(P(|X - \mu| \le k\sigma)\) is the proportion of data within k standard deviations of the mean.
  • \(\mu\) is the mean of the distribution.
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of the distribution.
  • \(k\) is the constant that determines the number of standard deviations from the mean.

Graphical representation in data analytics and statistics plays a crucial role in the effective communication of complex data and insights. Here are some key aspects of its use and significance:

(k) What is z-score? Explain.

A z-score, also known as a standard score, is a statistical measure that quantifies the number of standard deviations a data point is away from the mean of a distribution. It is used to standardize and compare data points across different distributions with varying means and standard deviations. Z-scores allow you to understand how far a data point is from the average in terms of the spread of the data.

The formula to calculate the z-score for a data point x in a distribution with mean μ and standard deviation σ is:

\(z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\)

Where:

  • \(z\) is the z-score.
  • \(x\) is the individual data point.
  • \(\mu\) is the mean of the distribution.
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of the distribution.
