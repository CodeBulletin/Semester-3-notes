Nominal Measurement

This type of measurement involves categorizing data into distinct categories or groups without any inherent order or ranking. Examples of nominal measurements include gender (male/female), color (red/blue/green), or product categories (electronics/clothing/books).

Ordinal Measurement

Ordinal measurement involves categorizing data into distinct categories or groups with an inherent order or ranking. However, the differences between the categories are not necessarily equal. Examples of ordinal measurements include rating scales (1-5 stars), educational levels (elementary/middle/high school/college), or customer satisfaction levels (very dissatisfied/dissatisfied/neutral/satisfied/very satisfied).

Interval Measurement

Interval measurement involves assigning numerical values to data where the differences between the values are meaningful and consistent. However, there is no true zero point. Examples of interval measurements include temperature (in Celsius or Fahrenheit), calendar dates, or IQ scores.

Ratio Measurement

Ratio measurement is similar to interval measurement but includes a true zero point, indicating the absence of the measured attribute. In ratio measurement, the ratios between values are meaningful and consistent. Examples of ratio measurements include weight, height, time, or income.
