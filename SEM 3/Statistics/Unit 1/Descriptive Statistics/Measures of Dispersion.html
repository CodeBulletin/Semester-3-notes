Measures of Dispersion

Measures of dispersion, also known as measures of variability or spread, are statistical metrics that describe the extent to which data points in a dataset differ from the central tendency (such as the mean, median, or mode). These measures provide valuable information about the spread or variability of data points, helping to assess the level of consistency or spread-out nature of the data set. In simpler terms, they give you an idea of how much individual data points deviate from the average or typical value.

Common measures of dispersion include:

  1. Range: The difference between the maximum and minimum values in a dataset. It provides a simple measure of how far apart the extreme values are.
  2. Interquartile Range (IQR): The range between the first quartile (Q1) and the third quartile (Q3) of a dataset. It is a robust measure of dispersion that is less affected by outliers compared to the range.
  3. Variance: The average of the squared differences between each data point and the mean of the dataset. Variance considers all the data points and provides an understanding of their collective variability.
  4. Standard Deviation: The square root of the variance. It measures the average deviation of data points from the mean. It is one of the most widely used measures of dispersion.
  5. Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD): The average of the absolute differences between each data point and the mean of the dataset. MAD provides a measure of dispersion that is less sensitive to outliers compared to the standard deviation.
  6. Coefficient of Variation (CV): The ratio of the standard deviation to the mean, expressed as a percentage. It is used to compare the relative variability of different datasets with different means.

These measures of dispersion are important because they help to assess the reliability of summary statistics (like the mean) and provide insights into the distribution of the data. In practice, the choice of a specific measure of dispersion depends on the characteristics of the data and the goals of the analysis.
