Chebyshev's Theorem, also known as Chebyshev's Inequality, is a statistical theorem that provides a general and more conservative bound on the proportion of data points that lie within a certain number of standard deviations from the mean, regardless of the shape of the distribution. Unlike the Empirical Rule, which specifically applies to normal distributions, Chebyshev's Theorem is applicable to any type of distribution, whether it's symmetric, skewed, or irregular.

Chebyshev's Theorem states that for any data distribution (regardless of its shape):

At least \(1 - \frac1{k^2}\) of the data lies within \(k\) standard deviations of the mean, where \(k\) is any positive constant greater than 1.

Mathematically this can be expressed as

\(P(|X - \mu| \le k\sigma) \ge 1 - \frac1{k^2}\)

Where:

  • \(P(|X - \mu| \le k\sigma)\) is the proportion of data within k standard deviations of the mean.
  • \(\mu\) is the mean of the distribution.
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of the distribution.
  • \(k\) is the constant that determines the number of standard deviations from the mean.
