z-score

A z-score, also known as a standard score, is a statistical measure that quantifies the number of standard deviations a data point is away from the mean of a distribution. It is used to standardize and compare data points across different distributions with varying means and standard deviations. Z-scores allow you to understand how far a data point is from the average in terms of the spread of the data.

The formula to calculate the z-score for a data point x in a distribution with mean μ and standard deviation σ is:

\(z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\)

Where:

  • \(z\) is the z-score.
  • \(x\) is the individual data point.
  • \(\mu\) is the mean of the distribution.
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of the distribution.

The z-score provides important information:

  • If the z-score is positive, it indicates that the data point is above the mean.
  • If the z-score is negative, it indicates that the data point is below the mean.
  • The magnitude of the z-score indicates how many standard deviations the data point is from the mean. A larger magnitude implies a data point farther from the mean.

Z-scores are particularly useful in comparing data points from different distributions, allowing you to understand how unusual or typical a specific data point is within its distribution. They are also used in various statistical analyses, hypothesis testing, and quality control processes.
