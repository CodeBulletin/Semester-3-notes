FISHER's LSD

Fisher's LSD (Least Significant Difference) test is a post-hoc statistical analysis technique used in conjunction with ANOVA (Analysis of Variance). When an ANOVA indicates that there are significant differences among group means, Fisher’s LSD test is used to determine exactly which means are significantly different from each other.

Key Aspects of Fisher's LSD Test:

Purpose:

  • To make pairwise comparisons between group means after an ANOVA test has found a significant F-statistic.
  • To identify which specific group means differ from each other.

Procedure:

  • Conduct an ANOVA to assess whether there are overall differences among the group means.
  • If the ANOVA is significant, proceed with Fisher's LSD test to compare each pair of means.
  • The LSD test calculates the difference between each pair of group means and compares this difference to a critical value derived from the Student’s t-distribution.

Comparison of Means:

  • Compare the absolute difference between each pair of group means to the LSD value.
  • If the difference is greater than the LSD, the difference between those two means is considered statistically significant.

Assumptions:

  • The groups should be independent.
  • The populations from which samples are drawn should be normally distributed.
  • Homogeneity of variances (equal variances across groups).

Interpretation:

  • A significant result indicates that the two group means being compared are significantly different.
  • It’s important to interpret the results in the context of the number of comparisons being made.

Limitations:

  • Fisher’s LSD does not control the familywise error rate (the probability of making one or more Type I errors when performing multiple comparisons), which can lead to an increased risk of false positives, especially when the number of groups is large.
  • It is often criticized for being too liberal (i.e., too likely to find significance) in comparison to other post-hoc tests that control for Type I error rate more strictly, such as Tukey’s HSD or Bonferroni correction.

Applications:

Fisher's LSD is commonly used in experimental studies where researchers are interested in comparing multiple group means, like in agricultural, medical, or psychological research, especially when the number of comparisons is not too large.
