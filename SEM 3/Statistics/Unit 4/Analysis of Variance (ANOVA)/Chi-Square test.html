Chi-Square test


The Chi-Square Test for a Single Variance is a statistical method used to test hypotheses about the variance (\(\sigma^2\)) of a normally distributed population. This test is different from the Chi-Square Goodness of Fit Test and the Chi-Square Test for Independence. It specifically focuses on whether the population variance differs from a specified value (\(\sigma_0^2\)).

Hypotheses:

  • Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)​): The population variance equals a specified value, i.e., \(\sigma^2=\sigma_0^2\)​.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_a\)​): The population variance does not equal the specified value, i.e., \(\sigma^2\ne\sigma_0^2\)

Test Statistic:

The test statistic for this hypothesis test is calculated using the formula:

\(\chi^2 =\frac {(n-1)s^2}{\sigma_0^2}\)

where:

  • \(n\) is the sample size.
  • \(s^2\) is the sample variance.
  • \(\sigma_0^2\)​ is the hypothesized population variance.

Degrees of Freedom:

The degrees of freedom for this test are \(n-1\), where \(n\) is the sample size.

Assumptions:

  • The sample is drawn from a normally distributed population.
  • The sample is a simple random sample, meaning each member of the population has an equal chance of being included.

Procedure:

  1. Calculate the Sample Variance: Compute the variance  \(s^2\) of the sample data.
  2. Compute the Test Statistic: Use the formula to calculate the Chi-Square statistic.
  3. Determine the Critical Value: Find the critical value(s) from the Chi-Square distribution table for the chosen significance level (\(\alpha\)) and degrees of freedom.
  4. Decision Rule:
    • If the test statistic falls in the critical region (beyond the critical value), reject the null hypothesis.
    • If the test statistic does not fall in the critical region, fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Interpretation:

  • Reject \(H_0\): There is sufficient evidence to conclude that the population variance is different from the specified value.
  • Fail to Reject \(H_0\)​: There is not enough evidence to conclude that the population variance is different from the specified value.

Applications:

This test is useful in quality control, industrial processes, and other fields where variance is a measure of consistency or reliability.

Limitations:

  • The test is sensitive to the normality assumption. If the population is not normally distributed, the results might not be reliable.
  • It is not suitable for small sample sizes.
