"One-way ANOVA" and "Two-way ANOVA" are both statistical methods used to analyze the differences among group means in a sample. However, they differ in terms of the number of independent variables they can handle and the type of effects they can test.

One-way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance):

  • Purpose: Used when comparing the means of three or more independent (unrelated) groups.
  • Independent Variables: Only one independent variable or factor.
  • Example: Testing the effect of different diets (vegetarian, vegan, omnivore) on weight loss.
  • Analysis Focus: Determines whether there is a statistically significant difference among the group means.

Two-way ANOVA:

  • Purpose: Extends the one-way ANOVA by not just looking at one factor but two independent variables simultaneously.
  • Independent Variables: Two independent variables or factors.
  • Interaction Effects: Can test for interaction effects between the two variables, which is not possible in one-way ANOVA.
  • Example: Testing the effect of diet (vegetarian, vegan, omnivore) and exercise routine (low, medium, high intensity) on weight loss.
  • Analysis Focus: Determines if there are any significant differences due to the independent variables themselves (main effects) and if there is an interaction effect between these variables.

In summary, the key difference is that one-way ANOVA looks at the effect of one factor on a dependent variable, while two-way ANOVA examines the impact of two factors and their interaction on a dependent variable. This makes two-way ANOVA more complex and informative in scenarios where multiple factors might influence the outcome.
