Two Population Variances (F Distribution)

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical technique used to compare means of two or more groups to determine if there are any statistically significant differences between them. When comparing the variances of two populations, the F-test is commonly employed. The F-test is based on the F-distribution, which is a probability distribution that arises in the context of comparing variances.

Here's a basic outline of how the analysis of variance for two population variances using the F-distribution is conducted:

Null Hypothesis (H₀):

  • \(H_0:\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2\)
  • The null hypothesis assumes that the variances of the two populations are equal.

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁):

  • \(H_{\alpha}:\sigma_1^2 \ne \sigma_2^2\)​ (two-tailed test) or \(H_{\alpha}:\sigma_1^2 \gt \sigma_2^2\) or  \(H_{\alpha}:\sigma_1^2 \lt \sigma_2^2\)​ (one-tailed test)
  • The alternative hypothesis posits that the variances of the two populations are not equal.

Test Statistic (F-ratio):

\(F = \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}\)

  • Where \(s_1^2\)​ is the sample variance of the first population and \(s_2^2\)​ is the sample variance of the second population.

Degrees of Freedom:

  • Degrees of freedom for the numerator (\(df_1\)) is \(n_1-1\), where \(n_1\)​ is the sample size of the first population.
  • Degrees of freedom for the denominator (\(df_2\)) is \(n_2 - 1\), where \(n_2\)​ is the sample size of the second population.

Decision Rule:

  • If the calculated F-ratio is greater than the critical value from the F-distribution table at a given significance level (commonly 0.05), you reject the null hypothesis.

Interpretation:

  • If the null hypothesis is rejected, it suggests that there is enough evidence to conclude that the variances of the two populations are not equal.

Considerations:

  • The F-test assumes that the populations are normally distributed.
  • The samples are independent.
  • The populations have the same variance.

Example:

Let's say you have two sets of data (sample variances \(s_1^2\) ​and \(s_2^2\)​), and you want to test if the variances are equal. You calculate the F-ratio, compare it to the critical value from the F-distribution, and make a decision based on the significance level.

Keep in mind that this is a simplified explanation, and the actual calculation might involve nuances depending on the specific details of your scenario. Additionally, statistical software or statistical tables are often used to find critical values and make decisions in practice.
