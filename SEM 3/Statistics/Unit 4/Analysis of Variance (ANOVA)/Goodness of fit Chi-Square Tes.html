Goodness of fit Chi-Square Test

The Goodness of Fit Chi-Square Test is a statistical hypothesis test used to determine how well a set of observed frequencies fits or matches a set of expected frequencies. It's commonly used when dealing with categorical data to test whether the observed frequencies differ significantly from what would be expected under a specific hypothesis.

Key Concepts of the Goodness of Fit Chi-Square Test:

Purpose: To test the hypothesis that the observed frequencies in a categorical dataset are consistent with a set of expected frequencies derived from a theoretical distribution.

Chi-Square Statistic: The test is based on the Chi-Square statistic, calculated using the formula:

\(\chi^2=\sum \frac {(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i}\)

where \(O_i\) is the observed frequency for category \(i\), and  \(E_i\) is the expected frequency for category  \(i\).

Degrees of Freedom: Calculated as \(\text{number of categories}âˆ’1\). This is important for determining the critical value from the Chi-Square distribution.

Assumptions:

  • Observations are independent.
  • The sample size is sufficiently large (usually, each expected frequency should be at least 5).
  • Data is in the form of frequencies or counts of categories.

Procedure:

  • Step 1: State the null hypothesis (e.g., "The observed frequencies are consistent with the expected frequencies").
  • Step 2: Calculate the expected frequencies based on the null hypothesis.
  • Step 3: Compute the Chi-Square statistic.
  • Step 4: Compare the calculated Chi-Square statistic to the critical value from the Chi-Square distribution table based on the degrees of freedom and a chosen significance level (commonly 0.05).
  • Step 5: Make a decision (reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis).

Interpretation:

  • Reject the Null Hypothesis: If the calculated Chi-Square is greater than the critical value, it suggests that the observed frequencies are significantly different from the expected frequencies.
  • Fail to Reject the Null Hypothesis: If the calculated Chi-Square is less than or equal to the critical value, there is no significant difference between the observed and expected frequencies.

Applications:

  • Fit of Theoretical Distributions: Testing if a sample comes from a specific distribution (like normal, Poisson, etc.).
  • Homogeneity and Independence in Contingency Tables: Checking if two categorical variables are independent.
  • Population Genetics: Testing Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
  • Market Research: Understanding consumer behavior in different categories.

Limitations:

  • Sensitivity to sample size.
  • Cannot be used with very small expected frequencies.
  • Only applicable to categorical data, not continuous data.
