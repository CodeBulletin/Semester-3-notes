Sampling frame

A sampling frame refers to a list or a set of individuals, items, or elements from which a sample is drawn. It serves as a representation of the target population from which the sample is selected. The sampling frame should ideally include all the members of the population being studied and should be well-defined and accessible.

The quality of a sampling frame is crucial because it determines the extent to which the sample represents the population accurately. If the sampling frame is incomplete or contains inaccuracies, it may introduce bias into the sample, leading to results that do not accurately reflect the population.

Sampling frames can take various forms depending on the nature of the study. For example:

  1. List-based sampling frame: This involves using a pre-existing list or database that contains the names, addresses, or other relevant information of the population members. Examples include telephone directories, voter registration lists, or employee databases.
  2. Geographic sampling frame: In studies that focus on specific geographical areas, such as neighborhoods or cities, a geographic sampling frame can be used. This frame typically includes all the addresses or geographic units within the target area.
  3. Random digit dialing: This method is commonly used in telephone surveys. It involves generating random phone numbers and using them as the sampling frame. However, it is important to note that this method may exclude individuals without landline or mobile phone access.
  4. Stratified sampling frame: In some cases, the population may be divided into subgroups or strata based on certain characteristics (e.g., age, gender, income). The sampling frame would then consist of lists or databases specific to each stratum.
