Random sampling and non-random sampling

Random sampling and non-random sampling are two broad categories of sampling methods used in statistics. Here's an overview of each:

Random Sampling: Random sampling involves selecting individuals or items from a population in a way that gives each member an equal chance of being included in the sample. This method relies on chance and randomness to ensure that the sample is representative of the population. Random sampling methods include:

  1. Simple Random Sampling: Each member of the population has an equal probability of being selected. This can be done using techniques like random number generators or lottery methods.
  2. Systematic Sampling: In this method, the population is ordered, and every nth individual is selected to be part of the sample. The starting point is randomly determined.
  3. Stratified Random Sampling: The population is divided into subgroups or strata based on certain characteristics, and random samples are taken from each stratum in proportion to their representation in the population.
  4. Cluster Sampling: The population is divided into clusters or groups, and a random sample of clusters is selected. All individuals within the selected clusters are included in the sample.

Random sampling methods help ensure that the sample is unbiased and representative of the population, allowing for generalizations and inferences to be made.

Non-Random Sampling: Non-random sampling methods involve selecting individuals or items from a population using non-random techniques. These methods do not provide every member of the population with an equal chance of being included in the sample. Non-random sampling methods include:

  1. Convenience Sampling: Individuals or items are selected based on their easy accessibility or convenience. This method is often used for practical reasons but may introduce bias as it may not represent the entire population.
  2. Purposive Sampling: Specific individuals or items are selected based on predetermined criteria or the researcher's judgment. This method is used when certain characteristics or expertise are required for the study.
  3. Snowball Sampling: Initially, a small group of individuals is selected, and then additional participants are identified and recruited through referrals from the initial participants. This method is often used when studying hard-to-reach populations.

Non-random sampling methods can be useful in certain situations, such as exploratory research or when studying specific subgroups. However, they may introduce bias and limit the generalizability of the findings to the larger population.
