Confidence Interval for Estimating Population Mean µ when σ is Unknown / t-satistics


When the population standard deviation (σ) is unknown, you can estimate the population mean (µ) using a confidence interval based on the sample standard deviation (s) and the t-distribution. This method is used when you have a sample from the population but don't know the true population standard deviation. Here's how to calculate a confidence interval for µ when σ is unknown:

Collect Data: Start by collecting a random sample from the population of interest. The sample should be random to ensure it is representative of the population.

Determine Confidence Level: Decide on the confidence level you want for your estimate. Common choices are 90%, 95%, and 99%, but you can select any confidence level based on your requirements.

Calculate Sample Mean and Sample Standard Deviation: Compute the sample mean (x̄) and the sample standard deviation (s) from your sample data.

Determine the Degrees of Freedom (df): The degrees of freedom are based on the sample size (n) and are used when working with the t-distribution. The formula for degrees of freedom in this context is (n - 1).

Find the Critical Value (t): The critical value (t) is obtained from the t-distribution table or a statistical calculator, based on the chosen confidence level and degrees of freedom. For a 95% confidence level and n degrees of freedom, you typically find the t* value.

Calculate the Standard Error (SE): The standard error is a measure of the variation in your estimate. It's calculated as:

SE = s / √n

Calculate the Margin of Error (ME): The margin of error quantifies the range within which you expect the population mean to fall. It is computed as:

ME = t* * (SE)

Construct the Confidence Interval: Use the sample mean (x̄) and the margin of error (ME) to create the confidence interval:

Confidence Interval = x̄ ± ME
