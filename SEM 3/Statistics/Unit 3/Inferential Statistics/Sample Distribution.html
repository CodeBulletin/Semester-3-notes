Sample Distribution

A sampling distribution refers to the distribution of a statistic (such as the mean or proportion) calculated from multiple samples of the same size drawn from a population. It provides information about the variability and characteristics of the statistic across different samples.

When it comes to large and small sample tests, it typically refers to the size of the samples used in hypothesis testing or statistical inference. Here's a brief explanation of each:

Large Sample Test ( \(n \ge 30\)): A large sample test refers to hypothesis testing or statistical inference conducted using a sample size that is considered large. In this context, "large" generally means that the sample size is sufficiently large enough to satisfy certain assumptions and approximations.

When the sample size is large, the sampling distribution of the statistic tends to follow a normal distribution, regardless of the shape of the population distribution. This is known as the Central Limit Theorem. As a result, statistical tests and confidence intervals can be based on the assumption of a normal distribution, allowing for more straightforward calculations and interpretations.

Large sample tests are commonly used in practice when the sample size is reasonably large, typically greater than 30. They provide reliable results and are often preferred due to the simplicity and robustness of the normal distribution assumption.

Small Sample Test ( \(n \lt 30\)): A small sample test refers to hypothesis testing or statistical inference conducted using a sample size that is considered small. In this context, "small" generally means that the sample size is relatively small, typically less than 30.

When the sample size is small, the sampling distribution of the statistic may not necessarily follow a normal distribution. In such cases, statistical tests and confidence intervals may need to rely on alternative distributions or non-parametric methods that do not assume a specific distribution.

Small sample tests require careful consideration and may involve more complex calculations or specialized techniques. They are often used when the sample size is limited due to practical constraints or when dealing with rare populations.
