Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution is a probability distribution that describes the number of successes in a fixed number of independent Bernoulli trials or experiments. Each trial has only two possible outcomes: success (usually denoted as "S") or failure (usually denoted as "F"). The binomial distribution is named after the mathematician Jacob Bernoulli.

Key characteristics of the binomial distribution:

  1. Binary Outcomes: In each trial, there are only two possible outcomes, often referred to as "success" and "failure." These outcomes are mutually exclusive.
  2. Fixed Number of Trials: The binomial distribution models a fixed number of independent trials or experiments, denoted as "n." Each trial is identical in nature.
  3. Constant Probability: The probability of success in each trial is the same and is denoted as "p." The probability of failure is therefore (1 - p).
  4. Independence: The trials are assumed to be independent of each other, meaning that the outcome of one trial does not affect the outcome of any other trial.

\(P(x) = {n \choose x}p^xq^{n-x}\)

\({n \choose x} = \frac{n!}{x!(n-x)!}\)

\(p + q = 1\)

\(mean(\mu) = n*p\)

\(var = n*p(1-p)\)

Binomial to Normal:

\(\frac{x - n*p}{\sqrt{n*p(1-p)}}\)
