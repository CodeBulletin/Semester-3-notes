Poisson Dirstribution

The Poisson distribution is a probability distribution that describes the number of events that occur within a fixed interval of time or space. It is often used in situations where events happen randomly and independently, and the average rate of occurrence is known. The distribution is named after the French mathematician Siméon Denis Poisson.

Key characteristics of the Poisson distribution:

  1. Discreteness: The Poisson distribution deals with discrete random variables, meaning it counts the number of events that occur, such as the number of phone calls received in a call center in a given hour.
  2. Independence: It assumes that events occur independently of each other within the specified interval. The occurrence of an event does not affect the probability of another event happening.
  3. Average Rate (λ): The Poisson distribution is defined by a single parameter, often denoted as λ (lambda), which represents the average rate of occurrence of events in the interval. This parameter determines the shape and characteristics of the distribution.

\(P(X =x) = \frac{\mu^xe^{-\mu}}{x!}\)

\(\sigma = \sqrt\mu\)

Common applications of the Poisson distribution include modeling the number of accidents on a stretch of road in a given day, the number of emails received in an hour, the number of customers arriving at a store during a specific time period, and many other scenarios involving random event occurrences.

The Poisson distribution is a useful tool in probability and statistics for analyzing and making predictions in situations where events occur randomly and independently, but the average rate of occurrence is known or estimated.
